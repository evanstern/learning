The one-liner

RAG grounds a model's answers in knowledge that lives outside its weights

A base model's knowledge is parametric — frozen into the weights at training time, lossy, and unattributable. RAG makes knowledge non-parametric: external, swappable, private, and citable.

RAG 101 · What it's for

The problem

Parametric knowledge is frozen, lossy, and can't cite itself

A model only "knows" what was baked into its weights at training time. That breaks down three ways:

  • Frozen — errata, a new sourcebook, today's data: invisible until you retrain.
  • Lossy — long-tail facts get smeared; the model recalls the gist, confidently wrong on specifics.
  • Unattributable — it can't tell you where it knows something, so you can't trust or verify it.
RAG's move: pull the relevant source text in at query time and hand it to the model as context — so the answer is grounded in something you can point at.

"Fewer hallucinations" is a tendency, not a guarantee — the model ignoring its context is a real failure mode (lesson 108).

RAG 101 · The problem

Why RAG, not something else

Three alternatives — and when each actually wins

Fine-tuning

Changes behavior

Teaches tone, format, task — not reliable facts. Re-freezes knowledge into weights and still can't cite. Bad at long-tail recall.

Long context

Stuff it all in

Works until it doesn't: cost, latency, and degraded attention over huge contexts. Doesn't scale past what fits — or what you can afford per call.

Tool / SQL

Structured lookups

Best when the answer is a precise query over structured data. RAG is for unstructured text where relevance is fuzzy.

The clean split: fine-tuning changes how a model behaves; RAG changes what it knows right now. They're orthogonal — you can do both. Don't fine-tune to inject facts.

RAG 101 · The pipeline

The most important consequence

Errors cascade downstream — so build retrieval-first

You can't prompt your way to context that was never retrieved. A miss upstream poisons everything after it.
  • Recoverable: right chunk buried in junk (low precision).
  • Unrecoverable: right chunk never reaches the model.

Build order = pipeline order — retrieval (102–105) before any generation prompt.

…and now the flip side

What got killed as folk wisdom

  • "Optimal chunk size is 200–500 tokens"
    — no primary source; entirely dataset-dependent.
  • "400–512 tokens, 10–20% overlap is the default best practice"
    — repeated everywhere, backed by no controlled benchmark.
  • "Reranking improves results 15–25%"
    — a real effect, but that precise range isn't reproducible.
  • "RAG fails at retrieval 40% of the time"
    — cited constantly, traceable to no reproducible study.
Trust the architectural patterns; distrust the magic numbers — they're corpus-specific, so measure them on your data (lesson 107).
RAG 101 · Folk wisdom, killed

Locating a real design on the map

the-stacks' wiki router: pre-filter vs post-filter

v1 put a "librarian" wiki in front of the vector store to scope retrieval (ask about goblins → search the Monster Manual). Two ways to wire that:

Route-then-retrieve (pre-filter): pick the scope, then search inside it. Risk: a wrong route makes the right answer unreachable.
Retrieve-then-prune (post-filter): cast the wide net, then subtract junk. The wiki can only remove what was already found — never hide the answer.

"Use the risky component to subtract, not to add."

v1 flipped to post-filter and tested it. Whether routing earns its keep at all — versus just hybrid + rerank — is the live debate we reopen in lesson 106.

Why this corpus

D&D is a corpus that reveals RAG's failure modes

Standardized

Same template

Every monster: stats, lair, behavior. Shared boilerplate phrasing → makes stop-word pollution visible.

Varied

Different substance

Wildly different content under identical structure — exactly the spread that exposes weak retrieval.

Identifier-dense

Proper nouns + tables

Githyanki, spell names, stat-block tables — where dense embeddings are weakest and BM25 shines (lesson 104).

Not luck — a corpus that's rigidly structured but wildly varied pre-loads the whole syllabus. The failures jump out fast and legibly.
RAG 101 · A revealing corpus

Seen, not asserted

The dumb embedder failing — live

query: "what environment do goblins make their home in?"

1 Githyanki ✕
2 Grimlocks ✕
3 Goblins — "lair in caves…" ✓
4 Stone Giants ✕
5 Goblin lairs ✓
  • Synonym blindness — "environment/home" vs "lair/caves"; bag-of-words has no semantics.
  • Stop-word pollution — function words dominate the vector; chunks win on shared grammar.
The LLM still answered from ranks 3 & 5 — but only because they reached it. Shrink top-k or grow the corpus and the answer silently disappears. Retrieval is the ceiling.
RAG 101 · The live failure

How we build it

A walking skeleton: stable interfaces, dumb-but-real bodies

  • Define the real interfaces (ingest / retrieve / generate) on day one — the shape is settled, so committing is low-risk.
  • Put the dumbest real implementation behind each — runnable end-to-end immediately.
  • Each later lesson swaps a body; the interface never moves. Nothing is thrown away.
Why not interfaces-only stubs? Because lesson 107's evaluation needs a runnable baseline from day one — "recall was X with the dumb retriever; hybrid took it to Y." A skeleton that runs makes the whole series measurable.

Bonus: a dumb embedder produces visibly bad search — which motivates the next lesson instead of us asserting it matters.

Key takeaways

What to walk away with

  • What it's for: knowledge outside the weights — current, private, citable. Not fine-tuning (that's behavior, not facts).
  • The cascade: retrieval is upstream of generation; you can't prompt your way to a chunk that wasn't retrieved.
  • Epistemic stance: trust the architectural patterns, distrust the magic numbers — measure on your own corpus.
  • Pre vs post filter: make a risky component able to subtract, never to gate.
  • Walking skeleton: stable interfaces + dumb-but-real bodies → runnable, measurable, nothing wasted.
