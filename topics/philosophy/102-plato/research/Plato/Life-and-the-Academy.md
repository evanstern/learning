# Life and the Academy

Plato (c. 428–347 BC) was an Athenian aristocrat on both sides — reputedly descended from Solon through his mother Perictione — whose family also supplied members of the Thirty Tyrants (his uncle Charmides; Critias, a relative, led them). His brothers Glaucon and Adeimantus appear as Socrates's interlocutors in the *Republic*. See [[_grounding]] Part 1 §1.

## The two formative shocks

The *Seventh Letter* (authenticity contested — Burnyeat and Frede against, Ledger and Kenny inclined toward; see [[_grounding]] Part 1 §2) describes a young man with political ambitions disillusioned twice: first when the Thirty tried to implicate Socrates in the arrest of Leon of Salamis, then when the restored *democracy* executed Socrates in 399 BC. Plato left Athens afterward, traveling to Megara and possibly Cyrene, Italy, Sicily, and Egypt.

## Syracuse: three failed engagements with power

Three voyages to Sicily — c. 385 BC (Dionysius I turned hostile; Dion became his devoted associate), 366 BC (tutoring Dionysius II, collapsing when Dion was exiled), and 361–360 BC (held against his will until Archytas of Tarentum interceded). Dion later took Syracuse by force and was murdered; the *Seventh Letter* addresses his followers. These are the only recorded attempts by Plato to apply his political philosophy to a live regime. See [[_grounding]] Part 1 §1.

## The Academy (c. 387 BC)

Founded at a grove sacred to the hero Academus outside Athens's walls: informal gatherings posing problems for common study (mathematics, dialectic, astronomy), charging no fees — not a degree-granting institution. The "let no one untrained in geometry enter" inscription is attested only ~700 years later and is unverifiable. Members included Aristotle (arrived 367 BC, stayed twenty years), Eudoxus, Theaetetus, and reportedly two women, Axiothea and Lasthenia. Speusippus (Plato's nephew) succeeded him; the institution's later history is covered in [[Legacy-and-Reception]]. See [[_grounding]] Part 1 §1.

## Dialogues, anonymity, chronology

Plato wrote only dialogues, never treatises, and never appears as a speaker; "strictly speaking, he does not himself affirm anything in his dialogues" (SEP). Every dialogue except the *Laws* features Socrates — but when Plato elaborates metaphysics or cosmology he moves Socrates aside for other leads (the Eleatic Visitor, Timaeus), one plank of the traditional view that the historical Socrates was a moralist only. See [[_grounding]] Part 1 §§2–3.

The conventional grouping (Vlastos): **early/aporetic** (Apology, Crito, Euthyphro, Laches, Protagoras, Gorgias…) — definition-seeking, ending in aporia; **middle** (Meno, Phaedo, Symposium, Republic, Phaedrus) — the Forms and constructive doctrine; **late** (Parmenides, Theaetetus, Sophist, Statesman, Timaeus, Laws). Stylometry secures a distinct six-dialogue late group and a few orderings, but SEP and recent scholarship caution that stylistic grouping ≠ philosophical grouping, and the whole ordering project is increasingly doubted (Kahn's unitarian challenge). See [[_grounding]] Part 1 §4.
