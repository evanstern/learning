# Legacy and Reception

## Aristotle's break

Aristotle spent ~20 years in the Academy (367–347 BC), then rejected its core: universals exist only *in* particulars — the form of apple lives in each apple, not in a separate realm (hylomorphism). He pressed the Third Man regress that Plato had raised against himself ([[Theory-of-Forms]]), and gave it its name. The tag "Plato is my friend, but truth is a better friend" is **not** in Aristotle: it paraphrases *Nicomachean Ethics* 1096a11–16 and reached its familiar Latin form via medieval biography and Cervantes. See [[_grounding]] Part 4 §1.

## The Academy's two endings (don't conflate)

After Speusippus and Xenocrates, the Academy turned **skeptical** (Arcesilaus c. 266 BC; Carneades from 155 BC). The institution effectively died in **86 BC** when Sulla's siege ravaged the site. The school **Justinian closed in 529 AD** was a Neoplatonic school founded c. 410 AD with no institutional continuity with Plato's — a standard conflation to avoid. See [[_grounding]] Part 4 §2.

## Transmission: Platonism's afterlives

Middle Platonism (from Antiochus, c. 90 BC) re-dogmatized the school and moved the Forms into a divine mind. **Neoplatonism** (Plotinus, c. 205–270; *Enneads*): the One beyond being, emanating Intellect (containing the Forms), World-Soul, world. Augustine reached Christianity through Latin Plotinus/Porphyry — evil as privation, Forms as God's ideas. Al-Farabi modeled his *Virtuous City* on the *Republic*; Ficino's 1484 Latin translation (Medici-sponsored) gave the Renaissance its complete Plato. See [[_grounding]] Part 4 §3.

## "Footnotes to Plato"

Whitehead, *Process and Reality* (1929): "The safest general characterization of the European philosophical tradition is that it consists of a series of footnotes to Plato." In context he credits "the wealth of general ideas scattered through" the dialogues — an inexhaustible mine of suggestion — not the systematic doctrine. See [[_grounding]] Part 4 §4.

## Popper's attack

*The Open Society and Its Enemies* vol. 1, "The Spell of Plato" (1945): the *Republic* as proto-totalitarian closed society, underwritten by historicism. Standard replies (Levinson, *In Defense of Plato*, 1953; Wild) charge Popper with ahistorical reading, tendentious translation, and deafness to the dialogues' irony; Popper answered in a 1961 addendum. See [[_grounding]] Part 4 §5.
