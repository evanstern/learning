# Theory of Forms

Plato's central middle-period doctrine: over and above changing particulars stand eternal, unchanging, non-spatiotemporal **Forms**, grasped by intellect rather than sense; particulars have their properties by **participation** (*methexis*) in them. See [[_grounding]] Part 2 §2.

## Motivation: three converging pressures

1. **Heraclitean flux** — the sensible world never holds still, so nothing sensible can be a stable object of knowledge.
2. **Parmenidean permanence** — what is truly real and knowable must be one, permanent, unchanging. The Forms concede Heraclitus about the sensible world while satisfying Parmenides about the intelligible one.
3. **The Socratic "What is X?"** — the definitional question seeks one universal answer, not a list of examples; the Form is what such a definition would be *of*. Aristotle reports this genealogy at *Metaphysics* 987b1–7.

See [[_grounding]] Part 2 §1.

## Epistemology: knowledge vs. opinion

*Republic* V: knowledge (*epistēmē*) is set over what completely is (Forms); opinion (*doxa*) over the in-between — sensible particulars, which both are and are not F. The **Divided Line** (VI, 509d–511e) grades cognition: *eikasia* (images) → *pistis* (physical objects) → *dianoia* (mathematics) → *noēsis* (Forms). The **Form of the Good** tops the hierarchy (Sun analogy, 508e): it makes the Forms knowable as the sun makes things visible. The *Meno*'s paradox of inquiry is answered by **recollection** (*anamnēsis*): learning is recovering what the soul knew before birth — demonstrated with the slave and the geometry problem. See [[_grounding]] Part 2 §3.

## Plato's own counterattack: the Parmenides

Plato subjected the theory to its hardest objections himself, in the *Parmenides* (130e–134e):

- **Participation dilemma:** does a particular get the whole Form (then one Form is wholly in many places, separate from itself) or a part (then the Form is divisible, hence many)?
- **Third Man Argument (132a–b):** from three tacit premises — *One-over-Many* (any plurality of F things has one Form of F), *Self-Predication* (the Form of F is itself F), *Non-Identity* (no Form is identical to its participants) — a regress: the large things plus Largeness₁ form a new plurality of large things needing a Largeness₂, and so on forever. Aristotle named it, restating it with "man."
- **The "greatest difficulty":** if Forms are wholly separate, we cannot know them at all.

Scholarly readings split: Vlastos saw "a record of honest perplexity"; others hold Plato intended readers to drop one premise and revise. Whether the *Timaeus* (which uses Forms robustly) is late (Cherniss — Plato kept the Forms) or middle (Owen — he could have abandoned them) remains unresolved. See [[_grounding]] Part 2 §4.
