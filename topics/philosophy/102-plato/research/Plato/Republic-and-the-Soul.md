# Republic and the Soul

The *Republic* answers Thrasymachus/Glaucon's challenge — show that justice pays *in itself* (Ring of Gyges: would anyone stay just if invisible?) — by building a city in speech to read justice "writ large," then mapping it back onto the soul. See [[_grounding]] Part 3 §3.

## The tripartite soul (Book IV)

Three parts: **reason** (*logistikon*, loves truth), **spirit** (*thumos*, loves honor; seat of anger and shame), **appetite** (*epithumētikon*, desires food/drink/sex/money). The argument runs on the **principle of opposites** (436b: nothing does or undergoes opposites in the same respect at the same time): the thirsty man who desires drink *and* holds back must contain two distinct sources of motivation; Leontius, furious at his own craving to stare at corpses, shows spirit is a third thing distinct from appetite (and from reason, which children and animals lack while having spirit). See [[_grounding]] Part 3 §1.

## The akrasia repair

In the *Protagoras*, Socrates denies weakness of will: no one goes willingly toward what they believe bad; "being overcome by pleasure" is a measurement error, curable by knowledge (Socratic intellectualism). Republic IV's divided soul **permits what the Socratic period denied**: appetite can defeat a rational judgment because it is an independent motivational source. The standard developmentalist reading (Vlastos; cf. Penner, Bobonich): Plato revising the master's psychology to fit the facts of inner conflict — with the refinement that partition most cleanly captures *impetuous* akrasia, while clear-eyed "standard" akrasia stays philosophically delicate even here. See [[_grounding]] Part 3 §2.

## Justice and the city–soul analogy

Kallipolis has producers, auxiliaries, guardians ↔ appetite, spirit, reason. Justice, in city and soul alike, is **each part doing its own work** (*to ta hautou prattein*, 433a–b); injustice is civil war among the parts. Justice pays because the just soul is ordered and free — the tyrannical soul is enslaved to insatiable appetite and most wretched (the three "proofs" of Books VIII–IX). Whether the analogy bears its argumentative weight is a live scholarly debate (Williams, Lear, Ferrari). See [[_grounding]] Part 3 §3.

## Philosopher-kings and the cave

Until philosophers rule, no rest from evils (473c–d): only they grasp the Forms and the Good ([[Theory-of-Forms]]), and they rule reluctantly. The **ship of state** (VI): quarreling sailors dismiss the true navigator as a star-gazer — philosophers' uselessness indicts the city, not philosophy. The **allegory of the cave** (VII, 514a–517a) dramatizes the ascent from shadows to sun (= Form of the Good); education is not inserting sight but **turning the whole soul around** (*periagōgē*, 518c–d). The returned prisoner is ridiculed, and the prisoners would kill whoever tried to free them (517a) — a transparent echo of Socrates's execution. See [[_grounding]] Part 3 §§4–5.

## The decline of regimes (VIII–IX)

Aristocracy → timocracy → oligarchy → democracy → tyranny, each with a matching soul-type, each transition driven by corrupted appetites and failed education; the demagogue-tyrant grows out of democracy's excess of freedom. Not presented as strictly inevitable (SEP). See [[_grounding]] Part 3 §6.
