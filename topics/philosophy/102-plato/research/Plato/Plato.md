# Plato

Lesson-scope grounding branch for philosophy lesson 102: Plato (c. 428–347 BC) — Socrates's student who turned the master's unanswered "What is X?" questions into the West's first systematic philosophy, and founded the school that carried it. Deeper than the arc-level survey in the topic vault; serves the Socratic teaching session directly.

## Scope

**In:** life and the Academy; the dialogue form and the literary-vs-historical Socrates; dialogue chronology; the theory of Forms (motivation, doctrine, epistemology, Plato's own self-criticism in the *Parmenides*); the *Republic* (tripartite soul, akrasia repair, justice, philosopher-kings, cave, regime decline); legacy (Aristotle's break, Neoplatonism → Augustine, Whitehead, Popper).
**Out:** Socrates himself (101's lesson vault covers him); Aristotle's own system (103 candidate); late-dialogue logic/cosmology beyond what the Forms debate requires.

## What is known

- **The man and the school:** an aristocrat twice disillusioned by Athenian politics (the Thirty, then the democracy that killed Socrates), three failed engagements with Syracuse, and the Academy (c. 387 BC) — fee-less, problem-centered, twenty years of Aristotle. → [[Life-and-the-Academy]]
- **The doctrine:** eternal Forms answering the Socratic definitional question, motivated by Heraclitean flux vs. Parmenidean permanence; knowledge vs. opinion on the Divided Line; recollection — and the *Parmenides*'s participation dilemma and Third Man regress, Plato's own hardest objections. → [[Theory-of-Forms]]
- **The system applied:** the tripartite soul (which permits the akrasia the *Protagoras* denied), justice as each part doing its own work in city and soul, philosopher-kings, the cave with Socrates's fate encoded in its ending, and the five-regime decline. → [[Republic-and-the-Soul]]
- **The afterlife:** Aristotle's immanent-universals break, the Academy's skeptical turn and two distinct "endings" (86 BC vs. 529 AD), Neoplatonism into Augustine, Ficino's Renaissance transmission, Whitehead's footnotes, Popper's attack. → [[Legacy-and-Reception]]
