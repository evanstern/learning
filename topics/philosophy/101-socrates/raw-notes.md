### [1] The sophist problem — first pass
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** In a direct democracy where arguing well = winning in politics/courts, what's the danger of a paid "professional persuasion teacher" class?
- **A (gist):** It creates a ruling class of persuasive, already-wealthy elites — "no bueno."
- **Verdict:** partial
- **Note:** Learner immediately spotted the *inequality/power* angle (fees gate rhetoric to the rich — grounding: Protagoras out-earned ten sculptors, Hippias made ~30 years' craftsman wages in one Sicily trip). Missed (so far) the deeper *epistemic* angle: persuasion decoupled from truth. Follow-up counterfactual planned: would free sophist training fix it?

### [2] What's still rotten if persuasion is free
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** Counterfactual: if sophist training were free and universal, is the problem solved?
- **A (gist):** No — what's missing is a moral framework to base arguments on; you can argue anything about anything if you stop short of agreeing on definitions or axioms.
- **Verdict:** aha
- **Note:** Learner independently derived BOTH halves of Socrates's response to the sophists: (1) persuasion without a moral framework is empty (care of the soul / virtue), and (2) arguments are unmoored without agreed definitions — which is literally the elenchus's opening move ("What is X?"). Perfect bridge into the method. Not yet covered: the other alternatives of the day (tradition/piety, natural philosophy).

### [3] Running the Laches elenchus
- **Mode:** worked-example (learner-driven)
- **Q:** Given "courage = endurance of the soul," "courage is fine," "ignorant endurance is not fine" — where's the contradiction?
- **A (gist):** "The definition must expand to account for the new constraints/rules."
- **Verdict:** partial
- **Note:** Learner sees the *consequence* (definition fails, must be revised) but skipped stating the contradiction itself — and said "expand" where the actual repair move is to *narrow/qualify* (endurance → *wise* endurance). Next: have them state the clash explicitly (some courage would be not-fine), then reveal Socrates attacks the repaired version too → aporia.

### [4] Learner attacks the elenchus itself
- **Mode:** socratic (learner counter-attacked)
- **Q:** (asked to finish the Laches derivation)
- **A (gist):** Refused — "the logic is flawed": "courage = endurance" isn't necessarily a ⇔ equality, so ignorant endurance needn't count as courage; informal language can't carry the weight of convicting the general.
- **Verdict:** tangent (high-quality) / partial
- **Note:** Learner spotted the load-bearing assumption: the contradiction only fires if the definition is read biconditionally. Correction to deliver: Socrates's "What is X?" *demands* the biconditional (all-and-only); retreating to "courage is a *kind of* endurance" concedes you haven't defined it — elenchus wins either way. Also connects to Frede/non-constructivism in grounding: elenchus proves joint inconsistency, not which premise is false. Learner's "informality of language" complaint is ironically close to the sophists' own relativism — worth naming.

### [5] General conceded; Protagoras detour requested
- **Mode:** discussion
- **Q:** (pending trap Q: what did Socrates *prove* — which premise is false? do we know what courage is?)
- **A (gist):** Conceded the two-door trap on the general ("got it"). Didn't answer the trap Q yet; asked to hear more about Protagoras — "he sounds like a nay-saying-dick and I can relate."
- **Verdict:** tangent
- **Note:** Learner self-identifies with the relativist gadfly-adjacent posture — useful hook for teaching the Socrates-vs-sophist distinction later (Socrates annoys people *toward* truth, sophists *away* from it). Serve a short Protagoras aside, then re-pose the trap question. Careful: Encomium of Helen / On the Non-Existent = Gorgias, not Protagoras.

### [6] "I don't know" — learner lands in aporia
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** What did Socrates prove? Which premise was false? Do we know what courage is?
- **A (gist):** "I don't know... either you accept ignorant endurance as courage or you move the goalposts. He proved the general didn't have a full grasp of courage, by his own admission."
- **Verdict:** aha (learner doesn't know it yet)
- **Note:** Exactly right on both counts: (1) contradiction shows the *set* is inconsistent, not which member is false (non-constructivist reading, per grounding: Frede/IEP); (2) the only positive product is exposed ignorance. And the learner's own "I don't know" IS aporia — they're living the method in real time. Reveal that, then pivot to Delphic oracle / Socratic ignorance.

### [7] Oracle paradox — first pass
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** How can "no one is wiser than Socrates" and "Socrates knows nothing" both be true?
- **A (gist):** "No one knows anything."
- **Verdict:** partial
- **Note:** Got the first-order half (the interrogated experts also lacked knowledge) but that alone makes everyone *tied*, not Socrates *wiser*. Missing piece: the second-order asymmetry — others falsely believe they know (double ignorance); Socrates knows that he doesn't (simple/Socratic ignorance). Push with: "why wiser, not equal?"

### [8] Oracle paradox resolved
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** If Socrates and the politician both know nothing, what does Socrates have that the politician doesn't?
- **A (gist):** "Socrates just has the clarity of mind to know he knows nothing."
- **Verdict:** correct
- **Note:** Nailed the second-order asymmetry once pointed at the ranking problem. Now name the terminology: simple vs. double ignorance (IEP), "I know that I know nothing" as paraphrase (Apology 21d actual: "what I do not know I do not think I know"). Next checklist beat: why Socratic ignorance is a *starting point*, not a conclusion (vs. skepticism/nihilism).

### [9] Why ignorance is a starting point
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** Why isn't Socratic ignorance a dead end? What can the refuted general do Tuesday that he couldn't Monday?
- **A (gist):** "Just because you don't know doesn't mean there isn't an answer — things can be true. On Tuesday the general can start acting more courageous by being less stubborn."
- **Verdict:** correct
- **Note:** Two strong beats: (1) learner spontaneously rejected the relativist reading — "things can be true" — i.e., Socratic ignorance presupposes truth exists (the anti-Protagoras stance); (2) the "Tuesday" answer smuggles in virtue-as-knowledge: the general behaves better *because his beliefs improved*. Use that to spring the akrasia question (can you know the good and still do wrong?).

### [10] Learner rejects "no one errs willingly"
- **Mode:** socratic (learner defending a position)
- **Q:** Is akrasia real, or is all wrongdoing ignorance? Defend a side.
- **A (gist):** (1) Clarified truth stance: truth exists but language is too fuzzy and logic too rigid to carry it alone. (2) Disagrees with Socrates: people do wrong intentionally — but "a sane person doesn't do anything they can't trace to a desirable outcome for themselves"; cites contrarianism, world-burning edgelords.
- **Verdict:** partial (strong engagement; hasn't noticed the concession)
- **Note:** GOLD: learner's own principle — all sane action traces to a perceived desirable outcome — IS Socrates's eudaimonist premise. The arsonist pursues burning *as* desirable (apparent good), so on the learner's own account no one pursues the bad *as bad* — which is most of the Socratic argument. The real battleground (donut/akrasia: acting against your own all-things-considered judgment) was named but not defended. Spring the trap, then concede the historical verdict (Aristotle on akrasia; Plato's tripartite soul as the fix). Language-fuzzy/logic-rigid stance is coherent — note the elenchus is precisely natural language stiffened at the joints with logic.

### [11] Lollipop-theft gambit (dodging the donut)
- **Mode:** discussion (deflection)
- **Q:** (pending: the 2am donut — is judgment defied, or rewritten?)
- **A (gist):** Joke-deflected: Protagoras would steal a kid's lollipop just to prove Socrates wrong.
- **Verdict:** tangent
- **Note:** The joke is secretly a real move — the *demonstrative* counterexample (do wrong deliberately, as-wrong, to refute the thesis). But it self-defeats: the theft's goal is winning the argument, i.e., an apparent good, so it confirms Socrates. Same structure as the contrarian case from [10]. Deliver riposte, note that Plato staged this exact bout (the *Protagoras*, where the akrasia argument actually appears), then re-pose the donut. Learner has now dodged the introspective question once — watch for a second dodge; may be avoidance of the personal-report framing.

### [12] Donut answered: akrasia is real (and pathologized)
- **Mode:** socratic (learner commits to a position)
- **Q:** Donut at 2am: judgment overpowered (akrasia real) or judgment rewritten (Socrates right)?
- **A (gist):** Side 1, firmly: people knowingly and intentionally harm themselves — "we diagnose it as a mental disorder, but that's arguable."
- **Verdict:** correct (defensible; it's the historical majority view) + hidden irony
- **Note:** The irony to surface: calling clear-eyed self-harm a *disorder* half-concedes Socrates — a diagnosis says the action-guiding system malfunctions and needs treatment/instruction, not blame. That's Socrates's trial argument in modern clothes ("if I corrupted them, I did so unwillingly; instruct me, don't prosecute me" — IEP) and the ancestor of the therapeutic model (grounding: Stoicism → CBT lineage). Close the debate honestly: Aristotle rehabilitated akrasia; Plato split the soul into parts to explain inner conflict — even his own school walked it back. Learner held their ground through three Socratic pushes — good epistemic spine. Pivot to trial next.

### [14] Penalty phase — spirit right, stunt missed
- **Mode:** socratic (roleplay)
- **Q:** Convicted 280–221, prosecution proposes death — what does Socrates counter-propose?
- **A (gist):** "He accepts death rather than budge on his convictions."
- **Verdict:** partial (right spirit, missed the actual move)
- **Note:** Learner intuited the refusal-to-budge but not the Prytaneum provocation (proposes free meals for life, the Olympic-victor honor) nor the eventual 30-minae fine underwritten by Plato & friends, nor that the death vote passed by a LARGER margin than conviction (some acquitters flipped to death). "Unexamined life not worth living" (Apology 38a) belongs here — his reason exile is impossible. Next: Crito escape-refusal → personified Laws / social contract / never-return-wrong.

### [15] Crito refusal — practical half only
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** Why is escaping an unjust verdict wrong? (argument demanded, not "convictions")
- **A (gist):** Escape just relocates the problem — behave in Thessaly or run forever; "sounds like he had given up."
- **Verdict:** partial + misreading
- **Note:** Got the practical prong (exile is philosophy-incompatible, he'd re-offend anywhere) but missed the two moral prongs: (1) never do wrong even in return for wrong (Gorgias: better to *suffer* injustice than commit it — soul-damage asymmetry), (2) the personified Laws / proto-social-contract: 70 years of staying = consent; escape attacks the rule of law, not just the verdict; the *men* wronged him, the *laws* didn't. MISREADING to correct: "given up" — the Phaedo shows equanimity, not resignation (death = dreamless sleep or interrogating the heroes; last words "a cock to Asclepius" = death-as-cure reading). Distinguish despair from consistency.

### [16] Socratic problem — flat skepticism
- **Mode:** socratic
- **Q:** Given no writings and contradictory biased sources, what can we know about Socrates? Does it matter?
- **A (gist):** "We can't know much of anything, it's all secondhand from biased sources."
- **Verdict:** partial
- **Note:** Correctly states the Socratic problem (scholars: possibly unsolvable; "each age produces a Socrates of its own" — IEP). But it's flat — same pattern as the "language too fuzzy" move in [4]: a blanket verdict where the skill is *discriminating degrees* of evidence. Correction: triangulation still works — trial/death/399 multiply attested; the Apology was published while thousands of living trial witnesses could call foul (a real constraint); SEP ranks it most reliable source. And the philosophical content doesn't depend on attribution. Blanket skepticism is as cheap as blanket credulity — that's the teachable edge.

### [17] Why he matters — the Jesus parallel
- **Mode:** discussion (learner's own synthesis)
- **Q:** Make the case: why does a man who wrote nothing and proved nothing matter as much as the tradition says?
- **A (gist):** "Similarities to Jesus strike me... he himself may or may not have done anything, but enough people act as if he did that it doesn't matter anymore. What matters is we're still talking about Socrates today and there are 4 churches within 3 blocks of me."
- **Verdict:** aha
- **Note:** Learner independently drew the Socrates/Jesus parallel — wrote nothing, known only via devoted followers writing decades later, executed by the state, influence independent of historical recoverability. Antiquity drew it too: Mara bar Serapion (c. 73 AD) grouped Socrates among "three wise men" unjustly killed. The disanalogy to teach in the wrap: Jesus's legacy is a *revelation* (believe); Socrates's is a *method* (examine) — he's the founder whose churches are arguments (Academy, Lyceum, Stoa all claimed descent). Sociological answer given; philosophical "what changed" (cosmos→conduct, ethics as rational inquiry) delivered in wrap-up.

## Aha moments
- **[2]** Learner reinvented the "What is X?" definitional demand on their own — recognized that argument without agreed definitions/axioms is unconstrained. This is the hinge from "sophists are a problem" to "Socrates's method is the answer."
- **[6]** Learner answered "I don't know" to what the elenchus proves — which is both the correct technical answer AND the experiential point (aporia). The lesson performed itself.
- **[9]** "Things can be true" — learner spontaneously staked out the anti-Protagorean realism that separates Socratic ignorance from skepticism, right after having flirted with the relativist position themselves in [4].
- **[10→12]** Learner's own principle ("sane people only act toward perceived desirable outcomes") turned out to be Socrates's eudaimonist premise; their "we diagnose it as a disorder" turned out to be Socrates's instruct-don't-prosecute argument in modern clothes. Twice they articulated his machinery while arguing against him.
- **[17]** Independent Socrates/Jesus parallel (wrote nothing, follower-mediated, state-executed, influence detached from historical recoverability) — antiquity's own comparison (Mara bar Serapion), rediscovered unprompted.

## Misconceptions corrected
- **[3]** "The definition must *expand*" → a leaky definition must *narrow* (endurance → wise endurance); expansion admits more counterexamples, not fewer.
- **[7]** "No one knows anything" resolves the oracle → that alone leaves everyone tied at zero; the ranking requires the second-order asymmetry (simple vs. double ignorance).
- **[15]** "He had given up" → Phaedo evidence: argues philosophy until the end, drinks steadily mid-conversation, Asclepius joke (death-as-cure). Consistency, not resignation.
- **(standing)** "I know that I know nothing" is a later compression that appears nowhere in Plato; Apology 21d is "what I do not know I do not think I know," and he claims moral knowledge at 29b.
