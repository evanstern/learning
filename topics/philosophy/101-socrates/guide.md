## 1. The problem: a democracy armed with rhetoric

Fifth-century BC Athens was a radical direct democracy: any citizen could address the
Assembly, juries ran to ~500 members chosen by lot, and there were no professional lawyers
or judges. Structurally, that made persuasive speech the sharpest weapon in the city — you
argued your own case, and the better talker won.

The **sophists** sold that weapon. Traveling professional educators — Protagoras, Gorgias,
Hippias, Prodicus — taught rhetoric and argumentation to wealthy young men for spectacular
fees (Protagoras reportedly out-earned ten sculptors; Hippias made roughly thirty years of a
craftsman's wages in one Sicilian visit). Their training was explicitly two-sided: learn to
argue *either* position persuasively. Protagoras backed it with theory — "man is the measure
of all things," truth relative to the persuaded.

**The session's key move:** the learner first identified the *inequality* problem (rhetoric
as a purchasable ruling-class weapon), then — pushed with the counterfactual "what if the
training were free and universal?" — landed on the deeper rot in their own words: *what's
missing is a moral framework, and you can argue anything about anything if you stop short of
agreeing on definitions.* That second half is, almost verbatim, the opening move of the
Socratic method. Fairness note the lesson kept: the sophists-as-charlatans picture is largely
Plato's hostile portrait; Protagoras's claim that excellence is trainable rather than
inherited was genuinely radical, even democratic.

## 2. The man

Socrates of Athens, c. 470–399 BC. Stonemason's son, midwife's son (a fact he made
philosophical use of), hoplite veteran of three campaigns with a reputation for hardiness,
famously ugly — bulging eyes, snub nose — barefoot, one ragged cloak. He wrote nothing,
founded no school, and **charged no fees**: the deliberate inverse of the sophists. He spent
his days in the agora interrogating anyone — young, old, slave, free — about courage,
justice, piety, "the state of their souls." He claimed a private *daimonion*, an inner
divine voice that only ever said *no*.

## 3. The method: elenchus, aporia, and what refutation actually proves

The **elenchus** runs: demand a definition ("What is X?") → secure agreement to two or three
innocent-looking premises → show the premises jointly contradict the definition → the
definition falls. The dialogue typically ends in **aporia** — "no way through" — what Vlastos
called "an improved state of still not knowing what to say."

The session ran the reconstructed *Laches* example live:

1. Courage **is** endurance of the soul (the general's definition)
2. Courage is a fine thing
3. Ignorant endurance is not a fine thing

If courage just *is* endurance, then ignorant endurance is courage that isn't fine — premise
2 explodes. The repair must *narrow* ("wise endurance" — which Socrates also dismantles),
not expand; the learner initially said "expand," a corrected misconception worth keeping.

Two deeper beats from the session:

- **The learner attacked the method itself**, objecting that the contradiction only fires if
  the definition is read biconditionally. Correct — and that's the point: Socrates's "What is
  X?" *demands* the biconditional (the mark present in all and only cases of X). Retreat to
  "courage is a *kind of* endurance" and you've conceded you haven't defined it. The elenchus
  wins on both branches.
- **What did Socrates prove?** Asked this, the learner answered "I don't know" — which is the
  technically correct answer (a contradiction licenses only "not all three premises are
  true," never *which* one is false — the non-constructivist reading) and simultaneously the
  experiential point: the learner was standing in aporia while describing it. Nothing is
  built; the demolition is the product.

**Maieutics**, briefly: in the *Theaetetus* Socrates likens himself to his mother's
profession — a midwife of ideas, who helps deliver a belief and only *then* tests it by
elenchus to see if it's a live birth or a phantom.

## 4. Socratic ignorance: the oracle's riddle

Chaerephon asked the Delphic Oracle whether anyone was wiser than Socrates; the Oracle said
no one. Baffled — he was sure he knew nothing — Socrates treated the verdict as a puzzle and
cross-examined the reputedly wise: politicians, poets, craftsmen. All failed as the general
failed. But "no one knows anything" (the learner's first pass) only ties everyone at zero,
and the Oracle issued a *ranking*. The resolution is second-order: the others are ignorant
**twice** (they don't know, and don't know that they don't — *double ignorance*), while
Socrates is ignorant once (*simple ignorance*). That one increment of self-awareness is the
whole of human wisdom.

Precision footnotes the lesson enforced:

- "I know that I know nothing" **appears nowhere in Plato**; *Apology* 21d is "what I do not
  know, I do not think I know."
- It isn't absolute: at *Apology* 29b he claims some moral knowledge (that doing injustice is
  bad) and says he'd die for it. He then did.
- Socratic ignorance is **not skepticism**: "I don't know what courage is" only stings if
  there's a fact of the matter. The learner's own formulation — "things can be true" — is the
  anti-Protagorean stake in the ground. False certainty is the obstruction; aporia is site
  preparation.

## 5. The ethics: virtue is knowledge, and the akrasia fight

Socrates held that **virtue is knowledge** and that **no one errs willingly**: every sane act
aims at an *apparent* good, so wrongdoing is always a mistake about what is genuinely good —
never a choice of the bad *as bad*. This denies **akrasia** (weakness of will) outright.

The session staged the fight properly. The learner rejected the doctrine — then articulated
"a sane person doesn't do anything they can't trace to a desirable outcome for themselves,"
which *is* Socrates's eudaimonist premise; every counterexample offered (contrarian
wrongdoing, world-burners, a lollipop-stealing Protagoras) fed the doctrine, since each
wrongdoer pursues something valued. The real battleground is the quiet case: the 2am donut.
Is the all-things-considered judgment **overpowered** (akrasia real, Socrates refuted) or
quietly **rewritten** on approach — "one won't hurt," "I've earned it" — so the failure was
in the knowing (Socrates stands)? The learner committed to overpowered: people knowingly harm
themselves.

Honest scoreboard, as taught: history mostly sided with the learner — Aristotle rehabilitated
akrasia (*NE* VII), and Plato split the soul into three parts precisely so it could fight
itself. But the learner's own framing ("we diagnose it as a mental disorder") is Socrates's
trial argument in modern clothes — "if I corrupted the youth I did so unwillingly; instruct
me, don't prosecute me" — malfunction to be treated, not sin to be punished. And the doctrine
survives institutionally: CBT descends from the Stoics, who descend from Socrates; "fix the
belief and the behavior follows" is virtue-as-knowledge with a copay.

Related commitments covered: **care of the soul** over body and wealth ("virtue makes wealth
good," not the reverse); **better to suffer injustice than commit it** (*Gorgias* — injustice
damages the doer's soul).

## 6. The trial, 399 BC

**Context:** Athens lost the Peloponnesian War in 404; the Spartan-installed **Thirty
Tyrants** (led by **Critias**, a former Socratic companion) executed ~5% of the citizenry in
months; democracy returned in 403 with an **amnesty** barring prosecution for political
crimes. Socrates's other famous companion, **Alcibiades**, had defected to Sparta mid-war.
The city wanted him answered for; politics was legally unsayable.

**The workaround** (which the learner partially derived playing prosecutor): *asebeia*
(impiety) — two prongs: rejecting the city's gods and "introducing new divinities" (the
daimonion, weaponized) — plus **corrupting the youth**, where the jury hears "Critias and
Alcibiades." The ground was pre-poisoned by Aristophanes' *Clouds* (c. 423 BC), which had
caricatured Socrates as an atheist con-man when the jurors were young; Socrates says in the
*Apology* that the old rumors are his real accusers.

**Mechanics and numbers:** ~501 jurors, water-clock speeches, same-day verdict. Convicted
**280–221** — flip 30 votes and he walks. In the penalty phase (prosecution: death;
defendant counter-proposes; jury must pick one), he proposed **free meals for life at the
Prytaneum** — the honor reserved for Olympic victors — before a grudging fine of 30 minae
underwritten by Plato and friends. The death penalty then passed **by a wider margin than
the conviction**: jurors who had voted innocent voted death. His reason exile was
impossible: **"the unexamined life is not worth living"** (*Apology* 38a) — silence is death
anyway, and "I shall obey the god rather than you."

**The cell (*Crito*):** escape was arranged and expected. He refused on two moral arguments
beyond the practical one (Thessaly would be philosophy-less exile): (1) *never do wrong, not
even in return for wrong* — the verdict's injustice is sunk; only his next act is live; and
(2) the personified **Laws of Athens**: seventy years of staying was consent; persuade or
obey; dodge one verdict and no verdict binds anyone. The first draft of social-contract
theory. The *men* wronged him; the *laws* didn't.

**The death (*Phaedo*):** he spent the last afternoon arguing about the soul's immortality
and drank the hemlock himself, mid-conversation. Last words: *"Crito, we owe a cock to
Asclepius — pay the debt."* Asclepius is the god of healing, thanked on recovery; the
prevailing (still-disputed) reading is a deadpan exit joke — death as cure. The learner's
"sounds like he gave up" was corrected against this evidence: consistency, not resignation.

## 7. The Socratic problem: grading the evidence

Socrates wrote nothing. The sources: **Plato** (devoted student, own agenda, dialogues
written decades later), **Xenophon** (absent from the trial, wrote from hearsay; his
Socrates contradicts Plato's on details like accepting payment), **Aristophanes** (hostile
caricature). Scholars call the reconstruction problem possibly unsolvable; "each age
produces a Socrates of its own" (IEP).

The lesson's correction to flat skepticism ("we can't know much of anything"): **grade the
evidence instead of issuing blanket verdicts.**

- *High confidence:* existence, the public-interrogation method, wrote nothing, trial and
  hemlock in 399 — attested by sources hostile to each other; enemies are independent
  attestation (Aristophanes was mocking him onstage 24 years before the trial).
- *Middling:* the *Apology* specifically — published while hundreds of living witnesses
  could call foul; ranked our most reliable source.
- *Murky:* where Socrates ends and Plato begins (the Forms are almost certainly Plato).

Escape hatch: **arguments don't have biographies.** The elenchus stands or falls on its own
merits regardless of attribution.

## 8. Why he matters

The learner's own synthesis: the Jesus parallel — wrote nothing, known only through devoted
followers writing decades later, executed by the state, influence detached from historical
recoverability. Antiquity drew the same parallel (Mara bar Serapion, c. 73 AD, grouped
Socrates among "three wise men" unjustly killed). The disanalogy that completes the answer:
Jesus's legacy is a *revelation* — content to believe; Socrates's is a *method* — a practice
to perform. His churches are arguments: the Academy, the Lyceum, the Stoa, the Cynics, the
Skeptics — all claimed descent from him and contradicted each other, which is what you'd
expect when the inheritance is a question.

What concretely changed: philosophy's **subject** (cosmos → conduct; Cicero: he "called
philosophy down from the heavens"); philosophy's **standard** (define terms, follow the
argument, even into the hemlock); and the **martyr template** for free inquiry.

**Series arc:** Plato (102, next — the student in the courtroom) → Aristotle → the
Hellenistic schools, each carrying a piece of the Socratic inheritance.
