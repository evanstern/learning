# Socrates

Deep-dive grounding for lesson `101-socrates` of the educate `philosophy` series: the life,
method, ideas, trial, and sources of Socrates (c. 470/469–399 BC), the conventional starting
point of Western moral philosophy.

## Scope

**In:** Socrates' biography and Athenian context; the elenchus, Socratic ignorance, irony, and
maieutics; the ethical doctrines attributed to him; the trial, imprisonment, and death of 399 BC;
the Socratic problem of sources. **Out:** the wider arc of Greek and later philosophy
(series-scope vault at `topics/philosophy/research/`); Plato's and Aristotle's own systems beyond
what bears on reading Socrates; verdicts about lesson design (that's the educate project's job).

## What is known

- A stoneworker's son who fought at Potidaea, Delium, and Amphipolis, lived barefoot-poor by
  choice, refused fees, questioned everyone in the agora, twice defied unlawful orders, and was
  executed at ~70 — [[Life-and-Context]].
- His method: "What is X?" → agreed premises → contradiction → aporia; wisdom as knowing one's
  ignorance; virtue as knowledge; the unexamined life not worth living; better to suffer wrong
  than do it — [[Method-and-Ideas]].
- In 399 BC a jury of ~500 convicted him of impiety and corrupting the youth by ~30 votes and
  sentenced him to death after he proposed Prytaneum honors as his "penalty"; he refused escape
  on principle and drank the hemlock — [[Trial-and-Death]].
- He wrote nothing; Plato, Xenophon, Aristophanes, and Aristotle give conflicting portraits, and
  scholarship ranges from developmentalism (early dialogues ≈ historical Socrates) to the view
  that the historical man is unrecoverable — [[The-Socratic-Problem]].
