# Trial and Death

The events of 399 BC as the sources give them — charges, trial mechanics, defense, imprisonment,
and execution.

## Charges and court

Meletus (formal accuser), Anytus, and Lycon charged Socrates with *asebeia* — not acknowledging
the city's gods and introducing new divinities (the daimonion) — and with corrupting the youth.
A jury of ~500–501 citizens chosen by lot heard timed speeches and voted twice: guilt (~280–221,
a margin of 30 votes per Plato), then penalty — where death passed by a larger majority than the
conviction. The trial took one day, around May–June 399 BC. The political subtext: the 403
amnesty barred prosecuting his associations with Critias and Alcibiades directly, and decades of
prejudice from Aristophanes' *Clouds* caricature primed the jury. See [[_grounding]] Part 3.

## The defense and the counter-penalty

In Plato's *Apology* Socrates answers the old *Clouds* prejudice, tells the oracle story, calls
himself the god's gadfly on the sluggish horse of Athens, refuses appeals to pity, and rebukes
the jurors for prizing money over their souls. Convicted, he first proposes free meals in the
Prytaneum (an honor for benefactors and Olympic victors), rejects prison and exile, then offers
a fine (the sources' 3,000 drachmae and 30 minae figures are likely the same amount described
differently). The jury chose death. Xenophon's *Apology* differs on key points: Socrates'
boastful manner (*megalēgoria*) as strategy, no counter-penalty at all, and acceptance of death
as escape from old age. See [[_grounding]] Part 3.

## Crito: why not escape

Execution was delayed (~a month, traditionally for the sacred ship's mission to Delos). Crito
offered a funded escape to Thessaly. Socrates refused: injustice damages the soul more than
death; escaping would wrong the city; the personified Laws of Athens argue that remaining in the
city constitutes agreement to obey its judgments — an early social-contract argument — and
"neither to do wrong nor to return a wrong is ever right." See [[_grounding]] Part 3.

## The death scene and afterlives

In the *Phaedo* (mostly arguments for the soul's immortality), Socrates, about 70, drinks the
hemlock; numbness climbs from the feet while his mind stays clear. Last words: "Crito, I owe a
cock to Asclepius; will you remember to pay the debt?" — read variously as a mundane debt, or
thanks to the healing god for death as cure; genuinely unsettled. Later readings of the trial
split between democracy-condemns-free-thought (e.g., I.F. Stone's inversion: Socrates provoking
his own martyrdom) and Athens-had-its-reasons (Waterfield's scapegoat/reconciliation reading);
a 2012 mock retrial split 5–5 on guilt. See [[_grounding]] Part 3.
