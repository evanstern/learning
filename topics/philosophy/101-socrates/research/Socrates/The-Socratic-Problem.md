# The Socratic Problem

Socrates wrote nothing; everything comes through others. This note maps the sources, their
characters, and the scholarly approaches to reconciling them — which doubles as a beginner
lesson in weighing historical evidence.

## The four main witnesses

- **Plato** — student; wrote most of the Socratic dialogues; the most influential portrait;
  accurate on Athenian names and places, but Socrates is his literary spokesman and may carry
  Plato's own views.
- **Xenophon** — soldier-historian and student; *Memorabilia*, *Apology*, *Symposium*,
  *Oeconomicus*; a plainer, more conventional, "less ironic" Socrates; absent from the trial
  itself (his account is via Hermogenes), and some of his scenes could not have occurred as
  presented.
- **Aristophanes** — *The Clouds* (423 BC), the only portrait published in Socrates' lifetime:
  a hostile comic caricature running the "Thinkery," teaching the weaker argument to win —
  which Plato says fed the trial prejudice.
- **Aristotle** — born after Socrates' death; brief, secondhand, but unattached and therefore
  weighted: Socrates asked questions claiming ignorance, focused on ethics not nature, reasoned
  by analogy, and sought universal definitions.

Contradictions are concrete — e.g., Plato's Socrates refuses payment; Xenophon's *Symposium* has
him say students pay him. Other Socratics (Antisthenes, Aeschines, Phaedo of Elis…) wrote
dialogues too, mostly lost; the genre (*Sokratikoi logoi*) is recognized as literary, not
transcription. See [[_grounding]] Part 4.

## Scholarly approaches

- **Schleiermacher (1815/1818)** framed the modern problem; held only the *Apology* and *Crito*
  purely Socratic; demoted Xenophon.
- **Developmentalism (Vlastos' "grand hypothesis")** — early/elenctic dialogues ≈ the historical
  Socrates; middle/late = Plato's own doctrine; supported by stylometry (Campbell 1867), internal
  evidence, and Aristotle's testimony. (The "Socrates-E/Socrates-M" labels sometimes attributed
  to Vlastos were not confirmed in primary sourcing.)
- **Unitarianism (Kahn)** — the dialogues are one philosophical vision in a literary genre;
  only the *Apology* has real historical weight.
- **The skeptical/literary turn (Dorion, 2011)** — the historical Socrates is unrecoverable;
  study each portrait and Socrates' reception (*Nachleben*) instead.

## What's secure vs. contested

Secure: Socrates existed; he practiced public philosophical questioning; he was tried and
executed in Athens in 399 BC. Contested: his actual doctrines, his politics, and whether any
single source captures the man rather than its author's agenda. See [[_grounding]] Part 4.
