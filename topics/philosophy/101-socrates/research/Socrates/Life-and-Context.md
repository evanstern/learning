# Life and Context

What is known (and disputed) about the man and the Athens he lived in.

## The man

Born c. 470/469 BC in the deme of Alopece to Sophroniscus (a stoneworker) and Phaenarete
(a midwife); married Xanthippe; sources disagree on his sons (two or three) and a possible
second wife (Myrto). He lived in deliberate poverty despite an inheritance — one ragged coat,
barefoot, rarely bathed — was famously ugly by every ancient account (bulging eyes, snub nose,
satyr comparisons), and famously hardy (impervious to cold and alcohol). He refused payment and
insisted he was not a teacher — his standing contrast with the sophists. He questioned everyone
in the agora — "young and old, male and female, slave and free" — about virtue and the state of
their souls. See [[_grounding]] Part 1.

## Soldier and citizen

Hoplite in three Peloponnesian War campaigns — Potidaea (432, where he saved Alcibiades' life),
Delium (424, praised for his conduct in the retreat), Amphipolis (422). Two civic acts of
defiance became canonical: as prytanis in 406 he alone refused the illegal collective trial of
the Arginusae generals; under the Thirty Tyrants (404–403) he refused the order to arrest Leon
of Salamis for summary execution. He took neither democratic nor oligarchic sides and criticized
both. See [[_grounding]] Part 1.

## The oracle and the daimonion

Chaerephon asked Delphi whether anyone was wiser than Socrates; the oracle said no one. Socrates,
puzzled, tested it by examining the reputedly wise, concluded his wisdom lay only in knowing his
own ignorance, and took the examination of himself and others as a divine mission. He also
reported a daimonion — an inner voice since childhood that only ever deterred, never advised —
which kept him out of active politics (and later fed the "new divinities" charge). See
[[_grounding]] Part 1.

## Why 399 BC was dangerous

Athens lost the war (404), suffered the Thirty Tyrants — led by Critias, a former Socratic
associate — and restored democracy in 403 under an amnesty. Alcibiades' treason and Critias'
terror made Socrates' associations politically toxic; the amnesty barred political prosecution,
and the case against him arrived instead as impiety and corrupting the youth. See
[[_grounding]] Part 1 and [[Trial-and-Death]].
