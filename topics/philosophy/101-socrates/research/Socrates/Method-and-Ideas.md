# Method and Ideas

The elenchus, Socratic ignorance and irony, and the ethical doctrines attributed to Socrates.

## The elenchus, step by step

1. A "What is X?" question (piety, courage, justice); the interlocutor offers a definition.
2. Socrates secures agreement to further premises.
3. He shows those premises jointly imply the contrary of the original definition — contradiction.
4. The definition falls; the exchange typically ends in *aporia* (puzzlement) — in Vlastos'
   phrase, "an improved state of still not knowing."

Scholars dispute what the elenchus proves: constructivists say it can establish truth/falsity of
specific answers; non-constructivists (and Frede against Vlastos) say refuting a thesis doesn't
prove its negation — the method exposes ignorance rather than builds knowledge. The *Theaetetus*
adds the midwife (maieutic) analogy: Socrates helps deliver an idea, then the elenchus tests
whether it is genuine or a "phantom" — generation vs. testing. See [[_grounding]] Part 2.

## Socratic ignorance and irony

"I know that I know nothing" is a paraphrase — what the *Apology* (21d) has is closer to "what I
do not know I do not think I know." The claim sits inside the oracle story and coexists with
passages (29b–d) where Socrates does claim knowledge worth dying for, so scholars debate the
absolute reading. IEP's useful distinction: simple ignorance (aware of it) vs double ignorance
(unaware, believing one knows). His irony — professing ignorance while winning every exchange —
is read variously as meaning-inversion, self-deprecation, deceptive masking, or sincere double
meaning (Kierkegaard: irony as his whole persona). See [[_grounding]] Part 2.

## Ethical doctrines

- **Virtue is knowledge**; hence **no one does wrong willingly** — wrongdoing is intellectual
  error, a denial of akrasia (weakness of will).
- **Unity of the virtues** — wisdom, temperance, courage cannot exist separately.
- **Care of the soul** above body and wealth; "the unexamined life is not worth living"
  (*Apology* 38a).
- **Better to suffer injustice than commit it** (*Gorgias*); never return wrong for wrong —
  the principle behind refusing both the Thirty's order and, later, escape from prison.
- The **turn from nature to ethics** — Cicero: he "called philosophy down from the heavens."

See [[_grounding]] Part 2.

## Original elenchus vs. modern "Socratic method"

The classical elenchus refutes and induces aporia; modern "Socratic seminars" are collaborative
meaning-making with facilitating teachers, structured formats, and no aim of refutation — related
in name and question-driven spirit, different in mechanics and goal. See [[_grounding]] Part 2.
