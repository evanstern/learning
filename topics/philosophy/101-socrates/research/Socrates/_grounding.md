## Part 1 — Life and historical context

### Life Dates and Family

- Ancient sources place Socrates' birth at approximately 469 or 470 BC; Wikipedia gives "c. 470 BC" while the IEP and SEP both give "469 BCE" — sources disagree on the exact year. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- He died in 399 BC in Athens, executed by drinking poison hemlock. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- He was born in the Athenian deme of Alopece. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- His father was Sophroniscus, described as a stoneworker/stonemason. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- His mother was Phaenarete, a midwife. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- After his father's death, Socrates became his mother's legal guardian, per SEP. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- He married Xanthippe. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Sources disagree on his sons and any second marriage: Wikipedia lists three sons (Lamprocles, Menexenus, Sophroniscus) and mentions a possible second marriage to a daughter of the statesman Aristides, with the order of marriages unclear; IEP says Xanthippe bore Lamprocles, while a possible second wife, Myrto, was mother to sons Sophroniscus and Menexenus; SEP names only Lamprocles and Sophroniscus as sons and does not mention a second wife by name. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Wikipedia states Socrates inherited part of his father's estate, "as was customary," which secured him "a life reasonably free of financial concerns," despite his self-imposed poverty. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- IEP and SEP indicate he came from a non-wealthy, non-noble family and, rather than laboring at his father's trade, spent his time in the agora engaging in philosophical inquiry. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Social Position, Poverty, and Appearance

- Despite his parents' relatively comfortable position, Socrates himself lived in deliberate poverty and was "indifferent to material pleasures." [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- He reportedly "neglected personal hygiene, bathed rarely, walked barefoot, and owned only one ragged coat," wearing the same clothes day and night according to SEP as well. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Though poor, he attracted a following of wealthy young aristocrats, one of whom was Plato. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- He is described as "adamantly" insisting he was not a teacher, and refused payment for his philosophical activity — a direct contrast to the sophists, who charged fees for education. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Ancient sources consistently describe him as physically unattractive: Wikipedia notes a "flat turned-up nose, bulging eyes and a large belly"; SEP describes him as resembling "a satyr more than a man," with "wide-set, bulging eyes," a "flat, upturned nose," and "large fleshy lips like an ass"; IEP similarly notes exophthalmic (bulging) eyes and a snub nose likened to a pig's. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Despite his unkempt appearance, he showed notable physical hardiness, reportedly "impervious to the effects of alcohol and cold weather." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Military Service

- Socrates served as a hoplite (heavy infantry footsoldier) in the Peloponnesian War across three campaigns: Potidaea (432 BC), Delium (424 BC), and Amphipolis (422 BC) — note Wikipedia does not give a specific year for Delium, while SEP and IEP both date it to 424 BCE. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- At Potidaea, he saved the life (and armor, per SEP) of the wounded Alcibiades. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- At Delium, his conduct during the Athenian retreat reportedly earned praise from both the general Laches and Alcibiades. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- After the defeat at Amphipolis, Socrates apparently ceased military service. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Philosophical Practice

- Rather than founding a formal school, Socrates spent his time in "the marketplace and other public areas, conversing with a variety of different people—young and old, male and female, slave and free." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- He questioned citizens about fundamental matters including "courage, love, reverence, moderation, and the state of their souls generally," as well as virtue and knowledge more broadly. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- He accepted no payment for this activity and avoided formal teaching structures, distinguishing himself from the sophists who charged fees. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### The Delphic Oracle Story

- Socrates' friend Chaerephon consulted the Oracle at Delphi and asked whether anyone was wiser than Socrates; per Plato's *Apology* the oracle answered that no one was wiser. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Puzzled by this answer, Socrates interpreted it to mean that he alone possessed a kind of wisdom precisely because "he alone knew that he knew nothing" — i.e., that human wisdom is worthless compared to divine wisdom, and this realization convinced him his mission was to examine himself and others. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Wikipedia's fetched content did not include this Delphic oracle/Chaerephon narrative in the sections retrieved, though it does reference the Delphic aphorism "Know thyself" in connection with Socrates. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)

### The Daimonion

- Socrates claimed to experience a "divine and daimonic" inner voice or sign that, in his words, "always deters me from the course of action I was intending to engage in, but it never gives me positive advice." [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- This daimonion reportedly began in his childhood and is described by SEP as often prohibiting actions "unrelated to matters of right and wrong," and by IEP as "a sign or voice from his days as a child that accompanied him and forbid him to pursue certain courses of action." [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- This inner voice is described as having influenced his general avoidance of active politics. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)

### Civic Acts of Defiance

- **Arginusae generals (406 BC):** Serving as a prytanis (a member of the presiding committee of the Athenian boule/council), Socrates was reportedly the sole member among the fifty who refused to allow an illegal vote when the Assembly attempted to try six generals as a group rather than individually, as Athenian law required. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) — Wikipedia's fetched content did not include this episode. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- **Leon of Salamis (404–403 BC):** Under the oligarchic regime of the Thirty Tyrants, Socrates was ordered to help arrest the democrat Leon of Salamis for summary execution; he refused to obey, an act later characterized as civil disobedience — Wikipedia notes he "was the sole abstainer, choosing to risk the tyrants' wrath and retribution rather than to participate in what he considered to be a crime." [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Politically, Socrates "did not take sides in the rivalry between the democrats and the oligarchs in Athens; he criticized both." [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)

### Relationships with Alcibiades and Critias

- Alcibiades was a student/companion whom Socrates saved at Potidaea; their relationship was politically and personally ambiguous — sources note Alcibiades' attempted (and per SEP, unsuccessful) seduction of Socrates. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Alcibiades later defected to Sparta following the scandal of the Mutilation of the Herms (415 BC), and his erratic, damaging career made his earlier association with Socrates politically toxic. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Critias, another companion of Socrates, went on to lead the oligarchic regime of the Thirty Tyrants (404–403 BC), raising suspicion that Socrates had influenced or mentored a tyrant. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- IEP notes that Critias, as one of the Thirty, put forth a law prohibiting Socrates from conducting discussions with young men under the age of 30 — an apparent attempt to silence or intimidate him — but Socrates remained in Athens regardless. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Athens' Defeat and the Restored Democracy's Climate (leading to 399 BC)

- In 404 BC, Athens was decisively defeated by Sparta at the naval Battle of Aegospotami, after which Sparta besieged the city. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Following this defeat, the democratic government was replaced by a pro-oligarchic regime known as the Thirty Tyrants (404–403 BC). [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates) [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Democracy was restored in Athens in 403 BC, but political tensions remained high afterward. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Despite a general amnesty that legally barred prosecutions for actions committed under the Thirty, Socrates' accusers — Meletus, Anytus, and Lycon — framed their case against him in religious and moral terms (impiety/asebeia and corrupting the youth) rather than explicit political charges, which some scholars read as a way of indirectly punishing him for his associations with Alcibiades and Critias without violating the amnesty. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Socrates lived through the entirety of this arc — from the height of Periclean Athens (c. 450–430 BC) through the long Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC), the oligarchic revolution, the democratic restoration, and the resulting political instability — which SEP frames as directly shaping both his philosophical practice and his eventual fate. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Socrates reportedly declined opportunities to escape his sentence, reasoning that "neither to do wrong or to return a wrong is ever right." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

## Part 2 — Method and philosophical commitments

### Elenchus — Step by Step

- The elenchus typically begins with a "What is X?" question (e.g., "What is piety?", "What is courage?"), to which the interlocutor proposes a definition. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Socrates then secures the interlocutor's agreement to further premises — e.g., in the *Laches*-type case reconstructed by Gregory Vlastos: "Courage is endurance of the soul," then "Courage is a fine thing," then "Ignorant endurance is not a fine thing." [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- Through logical argument, Socrates shows the further premises jointly imply the *contrary* of the original thesis (e.g., that courage is *not* endurance of the soul), producing a formal contradiction. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- The interlocutor's original definition is judged to have fallen/been refuted as a result. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- The dialogue typically ends not in a positive answer but in **aporia** — puzzlement, described by Vlastos as "an improved state of still not knowing what to say about the subject under discussion." [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- Michael Frede challenges Vlastos's reading of what the elenchus proves: showing a thesis false does not thereby prove its negation true — the method reveals ignorance rather than establishes new knowledge. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- Scholars split on the elenchus's constructive power: "constructivists" hold it establishes the truth or falsity of specific answers, while "non-constructivists" hold it "establishes the falsity of the conjunction of W, X, Y, and Z, but not the truth or falsity of any of those premises individually." [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- The elenchus is often characterized as Socrates "confusing, stinging, and stunning his conversation partners into the unpleasant experience of realizing their own ignorance," which sometimes provoked genuine intellectual curiosity in place of false certainty. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Subject Matter — "What is X?" Questions

- Socrates (as Plato depicts him) examined concepts such as "the virtues of piety, wisdom, temperance, courage, and justice," typically via definitional "What is X?" inquiries. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- He questioned people about "courage, love, reverence, moderation, and the state of their souls generally." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- The *Euthyphro* and *Ion* are cited as early Socratic/elenctic dialogues; the *Parmenides* is noted as a later dialogue in which Parmenides turns Socratic-style questioning against the young Socrates' own theory of Forms. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- The elenchus and related method appear prominently across Plato's early ("Socratic") dialogues, including the *Apology*, *Crito*, and *Gorgias*. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Socrates engaged everyone in this style of dialogue — "young and old, male and female, slave and free" — seeking to help interlocutors "recognize on their own what is real, true, and good," rather than lecturing them. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Socratic Ignorance and "I Know That I Know Nothing"

- The popular phrase "I know that I know nothing" is a paraphrase, not a direct quotation; it does not appear in this condensed form anywhere in Plato's text. [Wikipedia: I know that I know nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_know_that_I_know_nothing)
- What Socrates actually says in the *Apology* (21d) is closer to: "I neither know nor think I know," rendered in one translation as, "I seem, then, in just this little thing to be wiser than this man at any rate, that what I do not know I do not think I know either." (*Apology* 21d) [Wikipedia: I know that I know nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_know_that_I_know_nothing)
- The claim is embedded in the Delphic oracle story: either Socrates or his friend Chaerephon asked the Oracle at Delphi whether anyone was wiser than Socrates, and the Oracle answered that no one was wiser. [Wikipedia: I know that I know nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_know_that_I_know_nothing); [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Socrates investigated the oracle's claim by cross-examining supposedly wise politicians, poets, and craftsmen, and found that they claimed knowledge they did not actually possess, whereas he "was aware of his own ignorance." [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- He interpreted the oracle's verdict as meaning his wisdom consisted solely in "knowing that I know nothing" of the truly important matters, unlike others who falsely believed they knew. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Complicating the "absolute ignorance" reading, at *Apology* 29b–c Socrates twice claims to know something specific, and at 29d indicates he would be willing to die for that knowledge claim — in apparent tension with total ignorance. [Wikipedia: I know that I know nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_know_that_I_know_nothing)
- Because of this tension, modern scholars debate whether the absolute "I know nothing" formulation should even be attributed to the Platonic Socrates. [Wikipedia: I know that I know nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_know_that_I_know_nothing)
- Socratic ignorance is distinguished from "double ignorance": simple (Socratic) ignorance is being aware of one's own ignorance, whereas double ignorance is not being aware of one's ignorance while believing one knows. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- W. K. C. Guthrie summarized the point: "the only way in which he was wiser than other men was that he was conscious of his own ignorance, while they were not." [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- NOTE: The URL https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_ignorance returned a 404 (no standalone article exists); material on the concept is drawn from the IEP and the "I know that I know nothing" Wikipedia article instead.

### Socratic Irony

- Socrates is described as claiming to "know nothing of importance" while paradoxically maintaining "the upper hand in every discussion" — this apparent contradiction is the basis of his famous irony. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- His irony is evident particularly in the *Euthyphro*, where, addressing a supposed expert on piety, Socrates offers excessive praise while simultaneously undermining the interlocutor's claims to expertise. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Scholarly interpretations of Socratic irony diverge: some read it as saying the opposite of what is meant; others as self-deprecation; others as "masking with the intent to deceive"; still others as a genuine double meaning in which Socrates is simultaneously sincere and ironic. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/); [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Kierkegaard argued Socrates was ironic not merely in his speech but fundamentally as a character/persona. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### The Midwife (Maieutic) Analogy — and How It Differs from the Elenchus

- In the *Theaetetus*, Socrates compares his philosophical practice to midwifery, identifying it with his mother's own profession. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- As an intellectual midwife, Socrates does not supply ideas of his own; instead he helps his interlocutor "give birth to an idea." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Only after the idea is "delivered" does Socrates test it via the elenchus, to determine whether the mental "offspring" is a genuine truth or a mere "phantom" (false belief). [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- This marks a functional distinction from the elenchus proper: maieutics describes the generative, midwife-like role of helping produce a belief, while the elenchus is the subsequent critical/refutative testing of that belief. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Ethical Doctrines: Intellectualism and the Unity of Virtue

- Socrates held that "virtue is knowledge" — that all virtues are essentially forms of knowledge. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates); [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- The corollary doctrine is that "no one errs/does wrong willingly": if someone acts wrongly, it is because they do not know what is right, since one cannot simultaneously know the right action and choose to violate it. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- This position amounts to a denial of akrasia (weakness of will) as a cause of wrongdoing — moral failure is an intellectual error, not a failure of will. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Consistent with this, in his defense against the corruption charge, Socrates reportedly argued: "if he corrupted them, he did so unwillingly; but if unwillingly, he should be instructed, not prosecuted." [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- The doctrine of the **unity of the virtues** holds that virtues such as wisdom and temperance cannot exist separately, because they share the same opposites; "one cannot possess wisdom without temperance and vice versa." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Because knowledge is unified, the virtues that are forms of knowledge are correspondingly unified. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)

### Care of the Soul and the Unexamined Life

- Socrates prioritized the soul over body and wealth, arguing (per the *Apology*) that "wealth does not bring about human excellence or virtue, but virtue makes wealth and everything else good." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- He described his mission as concerned with "the improvement of their, and all the Athenians', souls," not their bodies or property. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- His most famous ethical claim: "the unexamined life is not worth living for a human being" (*Apology* 38a). [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/); [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- This claim calls for continuous self-reflection and accountability regarding one's beliefs and values as a condition of a worthwhile human life. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Better to Suffer Injustice Than Commit It

- In the *Gorgias*, Socrates argues that committing injustice damages one's soul more severely than suffering injustice does. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- He argues that punishment for wrongdoing actually purifies the corrupted soul, making it (in a sense) beneficial to the wrongdoer. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Consistent with this, Socrates is described as refusing to commit injustice "not even to injure in return for an injury received," and resisting orders/pressure to participate in an unjust execution (the arrest of Leon of Salamis) even at personal risk. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- More broadly, he maintained one should "never act unjustly, even to repay a wrong that has been done to oneself," subordinating self-interest/advantage to moral integrity. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)

### Turn from Natural Philosophy to Ethics

- Socrates explicitly denied studying "the heavens and what is below the earth," distancing himself from the cosmological concerns of the Presocratic natural philosophers and from the Sophists. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/); [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- His stated concern was exclusively ethical: what makes a person virtuous, and how one should live, rather than cosmological or natural-philosophical inquiry. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Cicero is credited with the summary judgment that Socrates "for the first time called philosophy down from the heavens, set her in cities, introduced her into homes, and taught her to inquire into life and morals, good and evil" — the classical statement of Socrates' reorientation of philosophy from cosmology to ethics. [BYU Studies (via web search; commonly cited to Cicero's *Tusculan Disputations*)](https://byustudies.byu.edu/article/socrates-mission)
- This shift established ethics as the central concern for the subsequent philosophical tradition running through Plato and the schools that followed. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)

### Modern Pedagogical "Socratic Method" vs. the Original Elenchus

- The classical elenchus was oriented toward refutation and exposing ignorance, challenging an interlocutor's implicit moral/epistemological beliefs, and often ending in aporia rather than a positive conclusion. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- Modern "Socratic seminars" instead emphasize collaborative meaning-making: participants work together "to construct meaning and arrive at an answer," rather than one party refuting another. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- In modern classroom use, students lead the discussion and teachers facilitate rather than interrogate; the explicit goal is that it is "not for one student or one group to 'win the argument.'" [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- Modern applications use structured pedagogical formats (e.g., inner/outer circles, triads, simultaneous seminars) in place of the spontaneous one-on-one dialogue of the classical elenchus. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- Modern Socratic-seminar questions are typically classified into opening, guiding, and closing questions, rather than being designed to induce aporia as in the classical method. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)
- The pedagogical rationale given for modern use is that "all new knowledge is connected to prior knowledge" and "all thinking comes from asking questions" — a constructivist-learning justification distinct from the classical elenchus's aim of refutation/self-knowledge. [Wikipedia: Socratic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_method)

## Part 3 — The trial and death (399 BC)

### Formal Charges and Accusers

- Socrates faced a formal charge of *asebeia* (impiety) with two prongs: "failing to acknowledge the gods that the city acknowledges" and "introducing new deities" (his personal *daimonion*, an inner voice that warned him against certain actions). [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- A second charge alleged corruption of the youth, i.e., that his philosophical questioning instilled moral nihilism or disrespect for authority in young Athenians. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Three men brought the prosecution: Meletus (the formal accuser who swore the charge and the only one to speak at trial), Anytus, and Lycon. [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato)) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- In the Apology, Socrates characterizes each accuser's motive: Meletus was "vexed on behalf of the poets," Anytus "on behalf of the craftsmen and politicians," and Lycon "on behalf of the rhetoricians" (23e–24a). [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- Anytus is described as a wealthy, prominent Athenian hostile to Sophists, who had returned from exile after the fall of the Thirty in 403 BC. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- One account holds that Lycon may have prosecuted partly because of Socrates' association with the pro-Spartan Thirty Tyrants, who killed his son Autolycus (disputed/uncertain attribution). [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))

### Legal Mechanics

- The jury consisted of approximately 500–501 male citizens selected by lottery from a larger pool of volunteers, a size intended to help ensure a decisive majority verdict. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Athenian trials of this kind used a two-vote system: a first vote determined guilt or innocence, and — only if guilty — a second vote determined the penalty, with prosecution and defendant each proposing a sentence. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- Speeches were timed using water clocks, standard Athenian legal procedure. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- The guilt vote: 280 jurors voted guilty, 221 voted for acquittal (commonly cited figures; some ancient sources give slightly different totals, and Plato notes "if just 30 votes had been cast differently, Socrates would have been acquitted" — exact tallies vary by source). [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- The penalty vote (death) passed "by a greater majority than the majority by which he had been convicted," indicating some jurors who voted for acquittal nonetheless voted for death once Socrates proposed his counter-penalty. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- The trial is dated to roughly May–June 399 BC. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Socrates complained in the penalty phase about the compressed timeline of Athenian capital trials, saying that if it were the custom, as elsewhere, "that a trial for life should not last one but many days, you would be convinced" of his innocence. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Political Subtext

- Athens enacted an amnesty in 403 BC after the democracy was restored, pardoning actions taken "for political and religious" reasons before or during the rule of the Thirty Tyrants; the prosecution of Socrates in 399 BC arguably skirted this amnesty by focusing on his associations and character rather than explicit prior political acts. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- The Thirty Tyrants, an oligarchic regime installed by Sparta after Athens' defeat in the Peloponnesian War, executed a large number of Athenians (one estimate cited is roughly 5% of the Athenian population). [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Critias, a leading member of the Thirty, and Charmides were both former associates/students of Socrates; as oligarchs they had attempted to silence him by forbidding him "to speak to men under thirty." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Alcibiades, an Athenian general and close friend of Socrates, championed the disastrous Sicilian Expedition, in which "virtually the entire Athenian invading force of more than 50,000 soldiers and non-combatants" was killed or enslaved; his notoriety further tainted Socrates by association. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- In Plato's Symposium, Alcibiades playfully "accuses" Socrates of arrogance before an audience, a scene later read as foreshadowing the real trial. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Aristophanes' comedy *The Clouds* (produced c. 423 BC) portrayed Socrates running a school teaching sophistic argument, including "dishonest techniques for avoiding repayment of debt" and encouraging young men "to beat their parents into submission" — a caricature that, per Socrates' own remarks in the Apology, had shaped the prejudices of jurors who "grew up believing the falsehoods attributed to him in the play." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/) [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Socrates held openly anti-democratic philosophical views, arguing that policy should be guided by "genuine knowledge and professional competence, which is possessed by only a few" rather than majority opinion, and he praised the political systems of Sparta and Crete — a stance some scholars connect to suspicion that his teaching bred oligarchic sympathizers like Critias and Alcibiades. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- A 2024 scholarly proposal (Matt Gatton) suggests the impiety charge specifically stemmed from Socrates' critique of the Eleusinian Mysteries, connected to Alcibiades' alleged profanation of the Mysteries (a minority/recent interpretation, not consensus). [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)

### Socrates' Defense in Plato's Apology

- The Apology is structured in three parts: Socrates' legal self-defense, the jury's guilty verdict, and the sentencing proceedings. [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- Socrates opens by addressing older, more diffuse prejudices against him (traceable to Aristophanes' Clouds) before responding to the formal charges of Meletus. [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- The oracle story: Chaerephon asked the Delphic Oracle whether anyone was wiser than Socrates, and the Oracle answered no; puzzled since he considered himself ignorant, Socrates set out to question politicians, poets, and craftsmen, discovering that each had false confidence in wisdom they did not truly possess, whereas he alone recognized his own ignorance — resolving the paradox but making him many enemies. [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- Socrates describes himself as a "gadfly" appointed by the god to sting the sluggish "horse" of the Athenian state: "All day long, I will never cease to settle here, there, and everywhere — rousing, persuading, and reproving every one of you." [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- He explicitly refused theatrical appeals for pity, declining to have his three sons brought into court to weep, stating in effect that he would rely only on argument and truth rather than "emotive tricks." [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- Regarding money and truth, Socrates rebukes the jurors: "Are you not ashamed that you give your attention to acquiring as much money as possible... and give no attention or thought to truth and understanding?" [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- On corruption of the youth, Socrates offers a dilemma-style rebuttal: if he corrupted young men, he must have done so unwillingly, and "if unwillingly, he should be instructed, not prosecuted." [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Socrates denied outright that he studied "the heavens and what is below the earth," distancing himself from the natural-philosopher caricature of the Clouds. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Plato does not provide the actual prosecutorial speeches of Meletus, Anytus, or Lycon, nor the names of any witnesses — the surviving text is entirely Socrates' side. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### The Counter-Penalty Episode

- After conviction, Socrates was invited to propose his own penalty as an alternative to the prosecution's demand of death. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- His first, provocative proposal was free meals for life at the Prytaneum, an honor normally reserved for state benefactors and victorious Olympic athletes. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- He then considered and rejected imprisonment or exile/banishment as penalties. [Wikipedia: Apology (Plato)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato))
- Sources differ on the exact fine figures: one account has Socrates proposing 100 drachmae (roughly one-fifth of his property), then a final compromise of 3,000 drachmae guaranteed by Plato, Crito, Critobulus, and Apollodorus; another (SEP) states he offered a fine of thirty minae — six times his net worth — with wealthy friends underwriting it. (Since 3,000 drachmae = 30 minae, these may be reconcilable descriptions of the same proposal stages rather than a true contradiction, but the sources present it differently.) [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- The jury rejected the proposed fine and selected the death penalty instead. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- One interpretation (SEP) suggests jurors may have feared divine retribution if they failed to execute a man already found guilty of impiety. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Delay, Crito, and the Argument for Staying

- Socrates was imprisoned for a period before execution — roughly four weeks per one source — traditionally attributed to the annual Athenian religious mission of a sacred ship to Delos (Delian mission), during which public executions were suspended to keep the city ritually pure. (The fetched Wikipedia Crito page did not itself explicitly corroborate the Delian-mission attribution, though it is the standard account, framed exactly this way in the Phaedo's opening.) [Wikipedia: Crito](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crito)
- In the dialogue Crito, Crito offers to bribe the guards and finance Socrates' escape to Thessaly, where friends would shelter him, arguing that Socrates' death would harm Crito's own reputation and deprive his sons of a father's guidance. [Wikipedia: Crito](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crito)
- Socrates refuses, arguing that a life corrupted by injustice ("damage to the soul") is worse than death, and that escaping would itself be an unjust act against the state that raised and educated him. [Wikipedia: Crito](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crito)
- He personifies the Laws of Athens as speaking to him directly, arguing that citizens who remain in the city (rather than emigrating) implicitly consent to obey its laws and judgments — an early articulation of a social-contract-style obligation — and that undermining a legal verdict, even an unjust one, would undermine the rule of law itself. [Wikipedia: Crito](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crito)
- Socrates also states to Crito a broader ethical principle: "neither to do wrong nor to return a wrong is ever right, not even to injure in return for an injury received," which he applies to refusing escape. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### The Death Scene (Phaedo)

- Socrates, then about 70 years old, drank the hemlock himself in accordance with the court's sentence rather than fleeing, consistent with his stated principle of civic obedience to law. [Wikipedia: Death of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Socrates)
- Plato's Phaedo describes the poison's progressive numbing effect moving upward through the body: an attendant checked his extremities and reported "his leg, and so upwards and upwards," showing he was "cold and stiff," while his mind reportedly remained clear until near the end. [Wikipedia: Death of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Socrates)
- A 19th-century physician, John Harley, self-administered a hemlock extract in 1869 and recorded symptoms (leg weakness beginning about an hour and a quarter after dosing, with mental clarity preserved) that corroborate the Phaedo's description. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [Wikipedia: Death of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Socrates)
- Socrates' last words, addressed to Crito, were: "Crito, I owe a cock to Asclepius; will you remember to pay the debt?" [Wikipedia: Death of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Socrates) [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Crito of Alopece took responsibility for Socrates' body after death and is said to have sacrificed the rooster to Asclepius on his behalf. [Wikipedia: Phaedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phaedo)
- The meaning of the final remark is disputed: interpretations include (a) a literal, mundane request to settle a household debt; (b) a symbolic statement of gratitude to Asclepius, god of healing, framing death as a "cure" or liberation from the illness/suffering of bodily life; (c) more generally, an expression of Socratic equanimity about death as either "a dreamless sleep" or "an opportunity to converse" with the dead in the underworld. No single interpretation is treated as settled among the sources consulted. [Wikipedia: Death of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- The Phaedo itself is largely devoted to Socrates' philosophical arguments for the immortality of the soul, discussed with companions Simmias and Cebes in prison, and concludes with a mythological account of the descent into Tartarus before the execution narrative. [Wikipedia: Phaedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phaedo)

### Xenophon's Differing Account

- Xenophon's *Apology of Socrates to the Jury* is a second, independent primary source on the trial, derived (since Xenophon was away serving as a Greek mercenary in 399 BC) from testimony given to him by Hermogenes, who was present at the trial. [Wikipedia: Apology (Xenophon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Xenophon))
- Xenophon frames Socrates' confident, even boastful courtroom manner (*megalēgoria*) as a deliberate rhetorical/legal strategy, in contrast to Plato's framing of Socrates' dignity as flowing from philosophical principle rather than tactics. [Wikipedia: Apology (Xenophon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Xenophon))
- In Xenophon's version, Socrates' willingness to accept death is grounded in a pragmatic argument that it is better to die now than to endure the "pains and limitations of advanced old age" — a notably different rationale from Plato's emphasis on divine mission and philosophical duty. [Wikipedia: Apology (Xenophon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Xenophon))
- Xenophon has Socrates refuse to propose any counter-penalty at all, on the grounds that proposing one would amount to admitting guilt — differing from Plato's Apology, where Socrates does propose escalating penalties (Prytaneum honors, then a fine). [Wikipedia: Apology (Xenophon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Xenophon))
- Xenophon reports the Delphic Oracle's verdict in different terms, saying Socrates was "more free, more just, or more sound of mind" than others, notably omitting the "wisdom" language central to Plato's version — possibly to distance Socrates from associations with atheistic natural philosophy. [Wikipedia: Apology (Xenophon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Xenophon))

### Later Interpretations

- Ancient reception: Marcus Aurelius reportedly criticized those responsible for Socrates' death as "lice"; Cicero attributed the conviction to Socrates' poor rhetorical strategy rather than the substance of the charges; the Syriac writer Mara bar Serapion (c. 73 AD) named Socrates one of "three wise men" unjustly treated by their societies. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Modern interpretations diverge sharply: I. F. Stone (1988) argued Socrates effectively sought death as a means of indicting Athenian democracy; Andrew David Irvine (2007) argued Socrates accepted the verdict out of loyalty to democratic and truth-telling principles rather than in defiance of them; Robin Waterfield (2009) framed Socrates' death as a quasi-scapegoat mechanism that helped "heal the City's ills" and enable postwar political reconciliation. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- A modern "mock retrial" held in 2012 with an international panel of judges reportedly split 5–5 on guilt, with those voting guilty nonetheless rejecting capital punishment as the appropriate penalty. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates)
- Broadly, the trial has been read in two competing frames across later ages: as an indictment of democracy for condemning free philosophical inquiry, versus a defense of Athens' democratic and legal norms against a figure who himself expressed anti-democratic sympathies and close ties to the oligarchic Thirty Tyrants. [Wikipedia: Trial of Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_of_Socrates) [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

## Part 4 — The Socratic problem: sources and how we know

### Socrates Wrote Nothing — The Problem of Sources

- Socrates left no written records of his own; everything known about him comes from secondhand accounts written by others, some of whom knew him and some of whom did not. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- The "Socratic problem" is the term for the difficulty of reconstructing a historical and philosophical image of Socrates from variable, and sometimes contradictory, ancient sources; scholars have called it "seemingly impossible to clarify and thus perhaps now classified as unsolvable." [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- As the IEP entry frames it, "each age, each intellectual turn, produces a Socrates of its own." [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Because sources are not consistently reliable, "finding the true Socrates or the original Socrates proves to be an impossible task"; scholars instead construct "a composite picture assembled from various literary and philosophical components." [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- A concrete contradiction across sources: Plato depicts Socrates refusing payment for teaching, while Xenophon's *Symposium* has Socrates stating that students pay him to teach wisdom. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- Beyond conflicting accounts, there is the additional problem of the fidelity of translation from classical Greek into modern languages. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)

### Plato

- Plato (c. 424/3–347 BCE) is the most frequently cited source and wrote the majority of Socratic dialogues, making him the most influential portrait of Socrates. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/); [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Plato was a student of Socrates and knew him mainly during the philosopher's later years; he shows remarkable accuracy regarding Athenian names, places, and relationships in his dialogues, but may have shaped Socrates' character to serve his own philosophical purposes. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Plato was roughly 42–43 years younger than Socrates; when Aristophanes wrote *The Clouds* in 423 BCE, both Plato and Xenophon were still infants. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- Plato cited Aristophanes' *The Clouds* as a contributing factor to the case against Socrates that led to his trial and execution in 399 BC. [Wikipedia: The Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Clouds)

### Xenophon

- Xenophon (c. 425–386 BCE) was a soldier-historian and a student of Socrates, said to have been recruited as a follower by Socrates in a narrow lane in Athens. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/); [Wikipedia: Xenophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophon)
- Xenophon wrote four Socratic works: *Memorabilia* (a defense of Socrates outside the courtroom), *Apology of Socrates to the Jurors* (his account of the trial defense), *Symposium* (Socrates and companions discussing what they take pride in), and *Oeconomicus* (household-management teachings). [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [Wikipedia: Xenophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophon)
- Xenophon was absent from Socrates' actual trial, being on campaign in Anatolia and Mesopotamia at the time; he was also only an infant during the events dramatized in his own *Symposium*, implying these are literary reconstructions rather than eyewitness reports. [Wikipedia: Xenophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophon)
- Xenophon's portrayal of Socrates is plainer and more conventional than Plato's — described as "duller, less humorous and less ironic than Plato's" — presenting Socrates as a practical and helpful advisor emphasizing self-mastery and self-sufficiency. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates); [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Both Plato and Xenophon wrote an *Apology* about Socrates' death but diverged in emphasis: Xenophon presented Socrates as handling his prosecution with arrogance, while Plato worked to temper that arrogance in his own account. [Wikipedia: Xenophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophon)
- Xenophon was historically dismissed by scholars as philosophically uninteresting, but recent scholarship has rehabilitated him as a legitimate source; modern scholarship has also found that some of Xenophon's Socratic accounts "simply could not have occurred as presented," suggesting he treated historical accuracy more loosely than in his properly historical works. [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- Beyond his Socratic writings, Xenophon achieved fame as a military commander who led the retreat of the Ten Thousand Greek mercenaries and as a historian of Greek affairs across several decades. [Wikipedia: Xenophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophon)

### Aristophanes and The Clouds

- Aristophanes (c. 450–386 BCE), a comic playwright, provides the earliest extant portrait of Socrates through his comedy *The Clouds*, produced in 423 BCE — the only portrait of Socrates published in his own lifetime. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/); [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- *The Clouds* premiered at the City Dionysia in 423 BC, finished last among competing plays, and was later revised (between 420–417 BC) and circulated in manuscript form. [Wikipedia: The Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Clouds)
- In the play, Socrates runs a school called the Thinkery ("Phrontisterion"), where students learn to "turn inferior arguments into winning arguments"; the school is depicted as disreputable, associated with "wastrels and bums" that respectable young men avoid. [Wikipedia: The Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Clouds)
- Socrates is shown suspended in a basket to better observe celestial phenomena, and is caricatured as "a petty thief, a fraud and a sophist with a specious interest in physical speculations" — including comic "discoveries" like measuring flea jumps with wax imprints and analyzing gnat buzzing via anatomy. [Wikipedia: The Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Clouds)
- Aristophanes' portrayal is considered a caricature that leans toward sophism and natural philosophy, making it uncertain how useful it is for historical reconstruction, though comedic exaggeration likely distorted the depiction significantly; Aristophanes was closer in age to Socrates and had direct exposure to him in his own younger years. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates); [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/); [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)
- The play represents early intellectual satire on the broader "battle of ideas" between traditional and innovative thought in classical Athens, with Socrates as its primary target. [Wikipedia: The Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Clouds)

### Aristotle

- Aristotle was born after Socrates' death (roughly 15 years after, per one account) and so had only secondhand knowledge of him, via Plato and other sources. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Because Aristotle lacked the personal/emotional attachment of Plato and Xenophon, his remarks are considered relatively unbiased, though brief and limited in scope. [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Aristotle explicitly distinguished Socrates from Plato: Socrates sought to clarify concepts in practical, everyday dialogues, whereas Plato connected concepts systematically to an ideal world of Forms. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- The IEP credits Aristotle with four specific, historically weighted observations about Socrates: that he asked questions while claiming ignorance, that he focused on ethics rather than nature, that he employed analogical reasoning, and that he pioneered the "what is it?" question seeking universal definitions. [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)

### Minor/Fragmentary Socratics and the Genre of "Socratic Dialogue"

- The Socratic dialogues collectively constitute a genre called *Sokratikoi logoi* ("Socratic dialogues") — reports/reconstructions of conversations involving Socrates, treated as the main sources for the historical figure. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- Besides Plato and Xenophon, other authors of Socratic dialogues include Aeschines of Sphettus, Antisthenes, Euclid of Megara, Phaedo of Elis, Aristippus, and Cebes; most of their work survives only in fragmentary form, though substantial portions of Aeschines' *Alcibiades* and *Aspasia* are extant. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- Antisthenes was a pupil of Socrates and was known to accompany him. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- The *logoi sokratikoi* are widely recognized by modern scholars as fictional/literary narratives rather than transcripts, which complicates any attempt to reconstruct an authentic Socratic philosophy from them. [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis: The History of the Socratic Problem"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)

### The Developmentalist Approach and Its Critics

- The standard modern (developmentalist) view holds that Plato's dialogues show a "development of thought from closer to that of Socrates' to a doctrine more distinctly Plato's own," typically divided by stylometric analysis into Early, Middle, and Late groups. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- Stylometric analysis as a methodological breakthrough is credited to Lewis Campbell's 1867 work; the chronological order of Plato's dialogues is not preserved even in ancient sources, and the dates Plato began writing and composed his last work are unknown. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- An opposing "Unitarian" position, associated with scholars like Charles Kahn, holds that Plato created his works not developmentally but as a unified philosophical vision, using the Socratic dialogue as a non-historical literary genre to express his own views throughout; Kahn separately suggested only Plato's *Apology* holds real historical significance. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [Wikipedia: Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates)
- John Cooper has argued against using chronological/developmental assumptions to prejudge interpretation, suggesting scholars examine each work individually while remaining free to reach varied, well-reasoned answers to "Who was Socrates really?" [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Landmark Scholarly Positions

- Friedrich Schleiermacher's 1815 essay (published 1818) is considered the most significant and influential founding statement of the Socratic problem; he maintained that only the *Apology* and *Crito* are "purely Socratic" and historically accurate, and he challenged reliance on Xenophon, arguing Xenophon "was not a philosopher but rather a simple citizen-soldier" while Plato offered a more philosophically compelling portrait. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [IEP: Socrates](https://iep.utm.edu/socrates/)
- Schleiermacher's methodological "golden rule" — establishing how Xenophon's portrayal must be supplemented to make sense of how Plato's portrayal was conceived — remains a standard comparative approach in Socratic studies. [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)
- Gregory Vlastos's "grand hypothesis" (developed roughly 1950s–1991) proposed that Plato's early "elenctic dialogues" (including *Apology*, *Charmides*, *Crito*, *Euthyphro*, *Gorgias*, *Hippias Minor*, *Ion*, *Laches*, *Protagoras*, and Book 1 of the *Republic*) preserve views closer to the historical Socrates, while middle- and late-period dialogues present views that are distinctly Plato's own; evidence includes stylometric data, internal cross-references, mentioned external events, doctrinal/methodological differences, and ancient testimony (particularly Aristotle's). [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [Wikipedia: Gregory Vlastos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_Vlastos)
- Vlastos's framework is sometimes described as distinguishing two different philosophical figures across Plato's corpus — an early, more historical Socrates and a later, more clearly Platonic Socrates; scholars who accept his framework are sometimes called "Vlastosians." NOTE: the specific labels "Socrates-E" and "Socrates-M" could not be directly confirmed in the sources consulted (Wikipedia's Vlastos article does not use this terminology); flagged as unconfirmed in primary sourcing. [Wikipedia: Gregory Vlastos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_Vlastos); [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)
- Louis-André Dorion, in "The Rise and Fall of the Socratic Problem" (Chapter 1 of *The Cambridge Companion to Socrates*, 2011), takes a skeptical, "literary turn" position: given the fictional nature of the *logos Sokratikos* and the wide divergences between Plato and Xenophon, he argues the search for the historical Socrates is futile, writing that "nothing justifies such a bias on the historical level" when privileging one ancient account over another. [Semantic Scholar: Dorion, "The Rise and Fall of the Socratic Problem"](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-Rise-and-Fall-of-the-Socratic-Problem-Dorion/a82650bc4abc5005e24c991ae6e0225ba59e1cf0); [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)
- Dorion illustrates the irreconcilability of sources with the "oracle story": Plato presents Socrates as an investigative seeker responding to the Delphic oracle, while Xenophon depicts him as someone who already possesses self-knowledge — divergent characterizations that cannot both be historically primary. [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)
- As an alternative to reconstructing "the" historical Socrates, Dorion proposes analyzing each Socratic work individually and shifting focus toward the *Nachleben* (afterlife/reception) of Socrates in later Socratic literature. [Semantic Scholar: Dorion](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-Rise-and-Fall-of-the-Socratic-Problem-Dorion/a82650bc4abc5005e24c991ae6e0225ba59e1cf0)
- Other historical milestones in the scholarship: G.E. Lessing sparked interest in the problem in 1768; Socher proposed a methodology in 1820; Søren Kierkegaard addressed the problem in *On the Concept of Irony with Continual Reference to Socrates* (1841); Karl Popper discussed it in *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945). [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- Twentieth-century scholarship split into two strains: "literary contextualists," who emphasize the artistic unity of the dialogues, and "analysts," who focus more heavily on the actual arguments; this tension continues to shape Socratic scholarship, alongside a 21st-century trend toward broader methodological consensus. [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem); [SEP: Socrates](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/socrates/)

### Secure vs. Contested Claims

- Debra Nails identifies four historically proposed (and still-debated) "solutions" to the Socratic problem for identifying the real Socrates: (1) the figure whose qualities are corroborated across Plato, Aristophanes, and Xenophon; (2) the figure who claims "to possess no wisdom" and engages in exercises for understanding; (3) Socrates as portrayed specifically in Plato's earliest dialogues; and (4) the individual who "turns from a pre-Socratic interest in nature to ethics, instead." [Wikipedia: Socratic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_problem)
- Claims considered most secure include: that Socrates existed, that he was tried and executed in Athens in 399 BC, and basic biographical/trial details corroborated in Plato's *Apology*. [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)
- Claims considered deeply contested include: Socrates' actual philosophical doctrines, whether he favored democracy or oligarchy, and whether any single source reliably captures his real teaching as opposed to reflecting its author's own agenda. [Antigone Journal: "Never-ending Crisis"](https://antigonejournal.com/2023/01/history-socratic-problem/)
