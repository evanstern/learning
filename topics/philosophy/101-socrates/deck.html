The Problem

Athens, 5th century BC: persuasion is power

  • Radical direct democracy.
    — any citizen speaks in the Assembly; juries run to 500; most offices filled by lottery ?.
  • No professional lawyers or judges.
    — you argue your own case in front of hundreds of neighbors. The better talker wins.
A system built on equal voice makes talking well the sharpest weapon in the city.

The Problem

The sophists ? sell the weapon

  • Traveling pros teaching the rich to argue either side of anything.
  • Protagoras: "man is the measure of all things" — truth is whatever persuades.

Rhetoric paid ~30 years of honest work per trip. The rich bought it.

The Problem

Even free persuasion is rotten

Suppose Athens made sophist training free and universal. Every citizen equally armed. Fixed?

  • No. Two things are still missing: a reason to care about truth over victory…
  • …and agreed definitions — stop short of those and you can argue anything about anything.
Socrates' whole method starts exactly there: force the definition into the open first.

The Method · worked example

A general defines courage — and loses it

P1 (the definition): Courage is endurance of the soul — standing your ground.
P2: Courage is a fine, admirable thing.
P3: Ignorant endurance — persisting cluelessly — is not fine.
Collision: if courage just is endurance, then ignorant endurance is courage — courage that isn't fine. P2 explodes. And the repair (“wise endurance”) must narrow, not expand… and Socrates dismantles that version too.
Socrates · Laches

The Method · the fine print

What did Socrates actually prove?

  • Only that the three can't all be true.
    — a contradiction licenses “not all of these” and nothing more. It never says which premise was false.
  • Nothing is built. The demolition is the product.
    — the general leaves knowing exactly one new thing: that he didn't know what he was sure he knew.
The correct answer to “so what is courage?” at the end of the dialogue is: “I don't know.” Saying it out loud is the skill.
Socrates · aporia

Socratic ignorance

The Oracle's riddle

  • His friend Chaerephon asks the Oracle at Delphi: is anyone wiser than Socrates? Answer: no one.
  • Socrates is baffled — he's sure he knows nothing of importance. But the god can't lie.
  • So he treats the oracle as a puzzle: he cross-examines everyone with a reputation for wisdom — politicians, poets, master craftsmen.
Every expert fails the way the general failed. So how can “no one is wiser” and “Socrates knows nothing” both be true?

Socrates · ignorance

Socratic ignorance

A starting point, not a shrug

  • Not skepticism. “I don't know what courage is” only stings if there's a real answer to be ignorant of. Things can be true.
  • False certainty is the obstruction. You can't fill a jar that thinks it's already full — aporia is site preparation.
  • Protagoras dissolves the question; the skeptic abandons it; Socrates keeps digging — because the answer exists and matters.

The ethics

Virtue is knowledge — no one errs willingly

  • Premise: every sane act aims at something the actor takes to be good for them. Even the arsonist pursues glory, revenge, the thrill — an apparent good.
  • So all wrongdoing is error — a mistake about what's genuinely good. Nobody reaches for the bad as bad.
  • Which means akrasia ? — knowing the good and doing the bad anyway — is declared impossible.
Socrates · virtue = knowledge

The ethics · stress test

The donut at 2am: two readings

Judgment overpowered.
“This is worse for me — and I'm biting anyway.” Verdict intact, defied. If this happens, akrasia is real and Socrates is refuted.
Judgment rewritten.
“One won't hurt… I've earned it… Monday.” The verdict was quietly hijacked before the bite. The failure was in the knowing — Socrates stands.
History mostly sided with the donut: Aristotle rehabilitated akrasia; Plato split the soul into parts so it could fight itself. But the doctrine survives — CBT's premise (fix the belief, behavior follows) is “virtue is knowledge” with a copay.

The trial · 399 BC

A traumatized city looks for someone to blame

  • 404 BC: Athens loses a 27-year war to Sparta. A Spartan-backed junta — the Thirty Tyrants — executes ~5% of the citizenry in months.
  • The junta's leader, Critias, and the war's great traitor, Alcibiades: both famously Socrates' companions.
  • 403 BC: democracy restored — with an amnesty: no prosecutions for political crimes. The city wants him gone, but politics is legally unsayable.
Socrates · context

The trial

The workaround: religion and the youth

  • Asebeia ? (impiety), two prongs: he rejects the city's gods, and “introduces new divinities” — his private daimonion ?, weaponized.
  • Corrupting the youth — the jury hears “youth” and thinks of two alumni: Critias and Alcibiades. Political rage, laundered as morals.
  • The ground was pre-poisoned: Aristophanes' comedy The Clouds had caricatured him as an atheist con-man — 24 years earlier, when the jurors were young.

The trial · penalty phase

His counter-offer: feed me at the head table

  • Athenian rules: prosecution proposes death, the convicted counter-proposes, jury picks one. Everyone expects exile.
  • His opening proposal: free meals for life at the Prytaneum ? — the honor reserved for Olympic champions. Only then a grudging fine, underwritten by Plato and friends. Too late.
  • Why not exile? “The unexamined life is not worth living.” Silence is death anyway; philosophizing elsewhere just restarts the trial abroad. “I shall obey the god rather than you.”
Socrates · Prytaneum

The cell

Crito arranges an escape. Socrates refuses.

  • Never do wrong — not even in return for wrong. Injustice damages the doer's soul; better to suffer it than commit it. “They started it” is not a moral operator.
  • The Laws of Athens speak: we raised you; 70 years of staying was consent; persuade us or obey us. Dodge one verdict and no verdict binds anyone.
  • The first draft of social-contract theory — residence as consent — two millennia before Hobbes. The men wronged him; the laws didn't. He declines to transfer the revenge.
Socrates · Crito

The hemlock

Consistency, not resignation

  • Spends his last afternoon doing what he always did — arguing philosophy (about whether the soul survives). Drinks the hemlock ? himself, steadily, mid-conversation.
  • His take on death: a dreamless sleep (no loss), or a relocation — in which case he'll spend eternity interrogating Homer and the heroes. “There, they surely don't kill you for it.”
Last words: “Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius — pay the debt.” Asclepius is the god of healing, thanked upon recovery. The prevailing reading: a deadpan exit joke — death is the cure.
Socrates · Phaedo

The catch

He wrote nothing. Grade the evidence.

High confidence: he existed, philosophized by public interrogation, wrote nothing, was tried and executed in 399. Sources hostile to each other — student, soldier, mocking comedian — all agree. Enemies are independent attestation.
Middling: the Apology — published while hundreds of living jurors could call foul. Our most constrained, most reliable source.
Genuinely murky: where Socrates ends and Plato begins. “Each age produces a Socrates of its own.” The saving grace: arguments don't have biographies — the elenchus works no matter who wrote it.

Why he matters

Churches that are arguments

Every school claimed him — and they contradicted each other. Exactly what you'd expect when the inheritance is a question, not an answer.

Socrates · legacy

Why he matters

Three things changed forever

  • Philosophy's subject: from “what is the cosmos made of?” to “how should a human live?” — ethics became a field you reason about. Cicero: he “called philosophy down from the heavens.”
  • Philosophy's standard: define your terms; follow the argument wherever it goes — even into the hemlock.
  • The martyr template: every thinker persecuted by the state since 399 BC stands in the shadow of that courtroom.
Socrates · what changed

Key takeaways

What to walk away with

  • The elenchus proves joint inconsistency — never which premise was false. Its product is honest ignorance, not answers.
  • Socratic ignorance is a ranking, not a shrug: knowing the limits of your knowledge is the whole of human wisdom.
  • “Virtue is knowledge” lost the akrasia fight — but survives everywhere beliefs are treated as the lever on behavior.
  • Everything we know is secondhand — so grade evidence; don't issue blanket verdicts. And arguments don't have biographies.
