# Before Socrates

The intellectual and civic world a learner needs before starting at Socrates: the early Greek
natural philosophers, the sophists, and 5th-century Athenian democracy.

## The pre-Socratics: from myth to nature

The defining project of the early Greek philosophers was replacing mythological explanations of
the cosmos with naturalistic, rational accounts, organized around two concepts: *physis* (nature)
and *arche* (origin/first principle). The Milesians of the 6th century BC began it — Thales
(water as arche; traditionally "the father of Western philosophy"), Anaximander (the *apeiron*,
the boundless), Anaximenes (air, transformed by condensation and rarefaction). Two poles then
framed Greek metaphysics: Heraclitus (everything flows; everliving fire; unity of opposites) vs
Parmenides (one unchanging Being; change is illusory; reason vs the senses). Pythagoras made
number fundamental; Leucippus and Democritus reduced reality to atoms and void. The label
"Pre-Socratic" is a 19th-century coinage and chronologically imprecise — Democritus was Socrates'
contemporary. Almost none of their writing survives directly; we know them through fragments and
*testimonia*. See [[_grounding]] Part 1.

## The sophists

Itinerant professional educators of the 5th–4th centuries BC who taught rhetoric, virtue (*arete*),
and much else for substantial fees — Protagoras ("man is the measure of all things"), Gorgias,
Hippias, Prodicus, Thrasymachus. They trained students to argue either side of a question and
engaged the *nomos*–*physis* (convention vs nature) debate. Socrates is distinguished from them
by refusing payment and questioning rather than instructing; their lasting "charlatan" reputation
descends largely from Plato's hostile portrayal. See [[_grounding]] Part 1.

## The Athenian stage

Athenian democracy (from Cleisthenes' reforms, 508 BC) ran on direct citizen participation — the
Ecclesia (assembly) and Boule (council of 500), offices filled by lot — which made rhetorical
skill practically valuable and gave the sophists their market. The Periclean golden age (445–429 BC)
gave way to the Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC), ending in Athens' total defeat — the backdrop of
Socrates' later life and trial. See [[_grounding]] Part 1.
