## Part 1 — Before and around Socrates: pre-Socratics, sophists, classical Athens

### Pre-Socratic Philosophy: What the Label Means

- The term "Pre-Socratic" was coined in the 19th century by German scholar J.A. Eberhard as *vorsokratische Philosophie* [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- The label is chronologically imprecise since several so-called Pre-Socratics (e.g., Democritus) were actual contemporaries of Socrates; "early Greek philosophy" is an increasingly preferred alternative term among scholars [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Little of the Pre-Socratics' original writing survives; what is known comes mostly through fragments quoted or paraphrased by later authors (*testimonia*) [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- These thinkers' defining project was replacing mythological explanations of the cosmos with naturalistic, rational accounts [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).

### The Milesians: Physis and Arche

- **Physis** ("nature") and **arche** ("origin/principle/first substance") are the foundational concepts pre-Socratic natural philosophers used to ask what the world is fundamentally made of and how it came to be [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Three philosophers from Miletus in the 6th century BC — Thales, Anaximander, and Anaximenes — initiated this naturalistic, monistic inquiry into a single underlying substance [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- **Thales** (c. 624–546 BC) is traditionally called the father of Western philosophy and held that the arche of the world is water [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Thales reportedly predicted a solar eclipse (585 BCE) and made early geometric discoveries, blending proto-scientific observation with his cosmological claims [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).
- **Anaximander** (610–546 BC) proposed the *apeiron* — an unlimited, undifferentiated "boundless" substance — as the arche, from which opposites like hot and cold differentiate [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Anaximander also suggested that humans descended from fish-like creatures, an early proto-evolutionary idea [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).
- **Anaximenes** (585–525 BC) held that air (*aēr*) is the primary substance, which becomes other materials through the processes of thickening (condensation) and thinning (rarefaction) [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).

### Heraclitus vs. Parmenides: Flux vs. Being

- Heraclitus of Ephesus is known for the doctrine of flux, summarized in the maxim *panta rhei* ("everything flows"), and the claim that one cannot step twice into the same river [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Heraclitus held that the cosmic order was not created by any god or man but has always existed as "everliving fire," while also affirming an underlying stability amid the constant change (unity of opposites) [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy); [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).
- Parmenides of Elea, by contrast, argued that only one unified, eternal, unchanging, and indivisible Being exists, and that change is illusory — a radically monist position that decisively shaped later Western metaphysics and ontology [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Parmenides distinguished between rational truth (the way of reason) and mere sensory illusion (the way of appearance), a distinction foundational to later epistemology [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).

### Pythagoras

- Pythagoras founded a cult-like philosophical/religious school that held numbers to be the fundamental constituents of the universe ("everything consists of numbers; the universe is made by numbers") [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Beyond the mathematical theorem bearing his name, Pythagoras taught the transmigration of souls (metempsychosis) and viewed the cosmos as fundamentally numerical and harmonious [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).

### The Atomists

- Leucippus and Democritus developed atomic theory, holding that only indivisible atoms and empty void ultimately exist [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy).
- Democritus argued that qualities like sweetness and bitterness are merely conventional, while atoms and void are the only true reality: "by convention sweet, by convention bitter... in reality atoms and void" [Wikipedia: Pre-Socratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy); [IEP: Presocratic Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/presocra/).

### The Sophists

- The term *sophistēs* derives from *sophia* ("wisdom"/"learning") and originally meant something like "sage" or "expert," before narrowing in the 5th century BCE to designate professional traveling educators [SEP: Sophists](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/).
- Sophists were itinerant teachers active in the 5th–4th centuries BC who taught rhetoric, philosophy, music, athletics, mathematics, and virtue (arete), chiefly to young statesmen and nobility, in exchange for fees [Wikipedia: Sophist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophist).
- Major named sophists include Protagoras of Abdera (ca. 490–420 BCE), Gorgias of Leontini (ca. 485–380 BCE), Hippias (a polymath), Prodicus (known for fine linguistic distinctions), and Thrasymachus [SEP: Sophists](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/).
- **Protagoras** is regarded as the first professional sophist; he argued that arete results from training rather than aristocratic birth, and is famous for the "Man the Measure" doctrine ("man is the measure of all things"), suggesting the subjective relativity of truth [Wikipedia: Sophist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophist); [SEP: Sophists](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/).
- **Gorgias** was known for rhetorical technique and for making unpopular positions appear persuasive; his works include *On the Non-Existent* and the *Encomium of Helen* [Wikipedia: Sophist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophist).
- Sophists charged substantial fees: Protagoras reportedly earned more than ten sculptors, including Phidias, while Hippias earned over 150 minas on a single visit to Sicily — roughly thirty years' wages for a skilled craftsman [SEP: Sophists](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/).
- Sophists trained students to argue persuasively on either side of a question and to speak convincingly even on unfamiliar topics, prioritizing persuasive effect over the pursuit of absolute truth [Wikipedia: Sophist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophist).
- The sophists engaged substantively with the *nomos*–*physis* (convention vs. nature) debate over whether moral and legal norms are natural or merely conventional [SEP: Sophists](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/).
- Socrates distinguished himself from the sophists chiefly by refusing payment for teaching and by using a questioning method (elenchus) rather than formal instruction [Wikipedia: Sophist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophist).
- Plato's dialogues portray the sophists negatively, as deceptive instructors focused on winning arguments rather than finding truth; this hostile portrayal heavily shaped the sophists' enduring reputation as "charlatans" rather than serious intellectuals [Wikipedia: Sophist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophist); [SEP: Sophists](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/sophists/).

### 5th-Century Athens: The Political Backdrop

- Athenian democracy was established in 508 BC under the reforms of Cleisthenes [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).
- Athenian government centered on two bodies: the Boule (a Council of 500, with 50 members selected by lot from each of the ten tribes) and the Ecclesia (the Assembly, open to all full citizens) [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).
- The Ecclesia functioned as both legislature and supreme court (except in murder and certain religious cases), and most public offices were filled by random lot (sortition) rather than election — the ten military commanders (strategoi) being a notable exception, chosen by election [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).
- Citizens participated directly in governance and in the courts, rather than through elected representatives — a structural reason rhetorical and argumentative skill (the sophists' stock-in-trade) was practically valuable for political and legal life [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).
- Public opinion among voters was also shaped by comic poets and political satirists performing in the city's theaters [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).
- Pericles dominated Athenian politics from 445–429 BC, a period of major cultural flourishing often called the "Age of Pericles," during which he commissioned the Parthenon and other major public works [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).
- The Peloponnesian War (431–404 BC) between Athens and Sparta ended in Athens's complete defeat in 404 BC, marking the city's decline and forming the direct historical backdrop to Socrates's later life and his execution in 399 BC [Wikipedia: Classical Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens).

## Part 2 — The classical triad: Socrates → Plato → Aristotle (survey)

### The Arc: Lineage and Periodization

- Socrates worked in the 5th century BC and marks a watershed after which Plato became his student and primary chronicler, forming a three-generation intellectual dynasty (Socrates → Plato → Aristotle). [Wikipedia: Ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy)
- Cicero characterized Socrates as having "brought philosophy down from the heavens, placed it in cities, introduced it into families," redirecting inquiry toward ethics and politics rather than cosmology. [Wikipedia: Ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy)
- Plato taught Aristotle at his Academy beginning around 367 BC. [Wikipedia: Ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy)
- Ancient Greek philosophy is periodized as Early/Pre-Socratic philosophy, then Classical philosophy (dominated by the Socratic school, i.e., Socrates/Plato/Aristotle), then Hellenistic philosophy after Alexander's era. [Wikipedia: Ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy)
- Aristotle's influence was initially eclipsed during the Hellenistic period but later profoundly shaped medieval and Islamic thought. [Wikipedia: Ancient Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy)

### Plato: Life, School, and Method

- Plato lived c. 428–423 BC to 348–347 BC in Athens, from an aristocratic family descended from Solon through his mother Perictione. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato)
- Plato was one of Socrates' devoted followers and became his most famous student; Socrates appears as the main speaker in nearly all of Plato's dialogues, though "Plato never speaks in his own voice" in them, and the exact relationship between Plato's literary Socrates and the historical Socrates is contested among scholars. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato)
- Around 383 BC, Plato founded the Academy near the sacred grove of Hecademus northwest of Athens; it began as "a house with a garden" and became a center for studying philosophy and mathematics until his death. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato)
- Plato innovated the philosophical dialogue as a literary and pedagogical form, using it to dramatize inquiry rather than present treatises; the dialogue form itself embodies the Socratic method of questioning and refutation. [IEP: Plato](https://iep.utm.edu/plato/)
- Plato synthesized and responded to earlier thinkers — Heraclitus's view of constant flux and Parmenides's metaphysical stability — by proposing that the sensory world is only an imitation of the eternal, unchanging world of Forms. [IEP: Plato](https://iep.utm.edu/plato/)

### Plato: Theory of Forms, the Soul, and the Republic

- The theory of Forms holds that the material world of constant change (perceived by the senses) is distinct from an unchanging realm of abstract Forms/Ideas (grasped by reason); Justice, Beauty, and mathematical truths exemplify these eternal Forms. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato)
- Under the theory of Forms, particular things "participate" in eternal Forms — e.g., individual beautiful objects derive their beauty through participation in the Form of Beauty. [IEP: Plato](https://iep.utm.edu/plato/)
- The theory of Forms is described as Plato's central doctrine: the perceptible world is "defective and filled with error," while a more real, perfect realm of eternal, changeless entities (Forms) exists beyond it. [SEP: Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/)
- In the Republic, Plato describes the soul as tripartite — reason (located in the head), spirit (upper torso), and appetite (middle torso) — paralleling society's three classes: rulers, warriors, and workers. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato)
- The Republic argues that justice in the soul consists in each of its three parts performing its proper function, with balance among reason, spirit, and appetite required. [SEP: Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/)
- The Republic proposes rule by philosopher-kings — rulers who combine wisdom (via understanding of the Forms) with virtue — as necessary for a just state. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato); [SEP: Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/)
- The Allegory of the Cave depicts prisoners who see only shadows, representing humanity's limited perception; the journey out of the cave toward sunlight symbolizes enlightenment through philosophical education. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato); [SEP: Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/)
- The Republic also describes a sequence of political decline — from aristocracy through timocracy, oligarchy, and democracy, to tyranny. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato)
- The Republic is considered Plato's most famous work, systematically integrating ethics, political philosophy, moral psychology, epistemology, and metaphysics into one interconnected system. [IEP: Plato](https://iep.utm.edu/plato/)

### Plato: Dialogue Chronology and Reading Order

- Scholars conventionally divide Plato's dialogues into three periods, though the chronology remains debated: Early (e.g., Apology, Crito, Euthyphro, Protagoras, Laches, Charmides) — focused on ethical questions without systematic doctrine and often ending in aporia (puzzlement); Middle (e.g., Republic, Phaedo, Symposium, Parmenides) — introducing the theory of Forms and positive teachings; Late (e.g., Laws, Timaeus, Sophist, Statesman) — sharing distinctive stylistic features and considered more philosophically challenging. [Wikipedia: Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato); [SEP: Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/)
- Beginners are commonly directed to start with the shorter, more accessible early dialogues — especially the Apology (regarded as "the most reliable of the ancient sources on the historical Socrates") and Crito — before progressing to longer works like the Republic. [SEP: Plato](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato/); [IEP: Plato](https://iep.utm.edu/plato/)

### Aristotle: Life and the Lyceum

- Aristotle lived 384–322 BC, born in Stagira in northern Greece; his father Nicomachus was a physician to the Macedonian king, which is linked to Aristotle's early interest in biology and medicine. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle)
- At about seventeen or eighteen, Aristotle joined Plato's Academy in Athens, earning the nickname "mind of the school," and remained there for nearly twenty years until Plato's death (c. 348/347 BC), after which he left Athens, partly due to anti-Macedonian sentiment. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- Aristotle returned to Athens in 335 BC and founded his own school at the Lyceum, named after Apollo's sacred grove; as a resident foreigner (metic) he could not own property and rented the building. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle)
- The Lyceum's members became known as "Peripatetics," from the colonnade (peripatos) where Aristotle lectured for roughly twelve years, conducting research with students such as Theophrastus; the school reportedly assembled antiquity's first major library. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)

### Aristotle: Break with Plato and Empiricism

- Aristotle rejected the existence of separately existing Forms, arguing that "universals still exist, but are only encountered when instantiated in a particular substance" — i.e., forms exist within individual things rather than in a separate realm. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle)
- Aristotle argued Platonists wrongly presumed single, univocal essences (e.g., of "goodness") where in fact the term applies differently across different cases (multivocity). [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- Aristotle held that mathematical and abstract objects are real but do not exist separately from sensible bodies, making his metaphysics more grounded in the observable world than Plato's. [IEP: Aristotle](https://iep.utm.edu/aristotle/)
- Aristotle was the first to study biology systematically, spending roughly two years observing zoology on Lesbos, gathering data systematically and inferring causal explanations from patterns — a marked contrast to Plato's emphasis on abstract Forms. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle)
- Aristotle is described as more empirically minded than both Plato and Socrates, combining logic with observation (including citing Egyptian/Babylonian astronomical observations and animal anatomy observed at sacrifices) to make general causal claims. [IEP: Aristotle](https://iep.utm.edu/aristotle/)

### Aristotle: The Four Causes, Logic, and Ethics

- Aristotle distinguished four explanatory causes for any change or thing: material (what it is made of), formal (its design/defining structure), efficient (the agent that brought it about), and final (its purpose or function); he held all four are needed for adequate explanation. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- In natural objects, the formal, efficient, and final causes typically coincide, since change originates internally. [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- Aristotle is credited with the earliest systematic study of logic, compiled into six books known as the Organon; his syllogistic logic — where the deductive validity of an argument can be determined by its structure rather than its content — remained the dominant form of Western logic until 19th-century advances in mathematical logic. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- The Nicomachean Ethics presents virtue as an excellence achieved through the "golden mean" — a moderate path between excessive and defective extremes of character. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- Virtue, per Aristotle, requires habituation through pleasure and pain — one becomes just "by doing just things" — and the good life consists of activities in accordance with excellence over a complete life. [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)

### Aristotle: Corpus and Reading Order

- Approximately one-third of Aristotle's original output survives (roughly 31 authentic works from an estimated original ~200 treatises), spanning theoretical sciences (physics, metaphysics, biology), practical sciences (ethics, politics), and productive sciences (rhetoric, poetics). [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/)
- Surviving Aristotelian texts are generally thought to be lecture aids/transcripts for students rather than polished publications intended for a general readership. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle); [IEP: Aristotle](https://iep.utm.edu/aristotle/)
- His most-cited foundational treatises include Physics, Metaphysics, Nicomachean Ethics, Politics, On the Soul, and Poetics, with the Nicomachean Ethics representing his practical philosophy and the Metaphysics his theoretical framework. [Wikipedia: Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle)
- None of the sources fetched gave an explicit, named "beginner reading order" for Aristotle (unlike Plato's Apology/Crito recommendation); the Nicomachean Ethics and Physics are simply the most frequently discussed entry-point texts. [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/); [IEP: Aristotle](https://iep.utm.edu/aristotle/)

## Part 3 — Hellenistic and Roman philosophy: the schools after Aristotle

### Historical Context: Why the Hellenistic Turn Happened

- Hellenistic philosophy is conventionally dated from Alexander the Great's death in 323 BC to the Battle of Actium in 31 BC. [Wikipedia: Hellenistic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy)
- Philosophical centers shifted from Athens toward Rome and Alexandria following Rome's military victories in the 2nd century BC. [Wikipedia: Hellenistic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy)
- The collapse of the independent city-state (polis) system meant citizens lost direct political agency, and "the greater insecurity and loss of autonomy of the era drove some to use philosophy as a means to seek inner security from the external world" (A.C. Grayling, as quoted). [Wikipedia: Hellenistic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy)
- Philosophy increasingly became practical and therapeutic — an "art of living" aimed at inner peace amid external chaos — rather than purely theoretical inquiry. [Wikipedia: Hellenistic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy)
- Epicurus expressed this therapeutic turn directly: "empty are the words of that philosopher who offers therapy for no human suffering." [Wikipedia: Hellenistic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy)
- The major schools of the period include Stoicism (Zeno of Citium, 3rd century BC), Epicureanism (Epicurus, 3rd century BC), two branches of Skepticism (Academic and Pyrrhonist), plus continuing older traditions such as the Cynics, Cyrenaics, Peripatetics, and the Academy (which evolved toward eclecticism). [Wikipedia: Hellenistic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy)

### Cynicism

- Antisthenes (c. 445–365 BC), a student of Socrates, founded Cynicism, emphasizing poverty and virtue over material accumulation ("I have enough to eat till my hunger is stayed, to drink till my thirst is sated"). [Wikipedia: Cynicism (philosophy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy))
- Diogenes of Sinope (c. 412–323 BC) is the dominant figure in Cynic history; he fled to Athens after a coinage scandal and, though possibly not directly trained by Antisthenes, adopted the ascetic Cynic lifestyle. [Wikipedia: Cynicism (philosophy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy))
- Diogenes was known for extreme practices (sleeping in a tub, eating raw meat) and embraced the nickname "the Dog," saying "other dogs bite their enemies, I bite my friends to save them." [Wikipedia: Cynicism (philosophy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy))
- Core Cynic doctrines: virtue as the sole necessity for happiness, pursued through asceticism (thin cloaks, going barefoot), rejection of social convention around wealth/power/fame, public shamelessness, and self-sufficiency. [Wikipedia: Cynicism (philosophy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy))
- Crates of Thebes (c. 365–285 BC), who renounced his fortune to live in poverty, taught Zeno of Citium, the founder of Stoicism — directly linking Cynicism to the origin of Stoicism; "the Cynic strain to be found in early Stoicism...can be ascribed to Crates' influence." [Wikipedia: Cynicism (philosophy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy))

### Stoicism

- Zeno of Citium founded Stoicism around 300 BCE in Athens, teaching at the Stoa Poikile ("painted colonnade"), from which the school's name derives. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)
- Zeno's ideas "developed from those of the Cynics," transmitted to him by Crates of Thebes, tracing a philosophical lineage back to Socrates via Antisthenes. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)
- Scholars divide Stoicism into three historical phases: Early/Old Stoa (Zeno through Antipater of Tarsus), Middle Stoa (Panaetius and Posidonius), and Late/Imperial Stoa (Musonius Rufus, Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius) — only complete Roman-era texts survive. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism); [SEP: Stoicism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/stoicism/)
- Chrysippus (c. 279–206 BCE), the third head of the school, was its foremost systematizer, particularly shaping Stoic logic. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism); [SEP: Stoicism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/stoicism/)
- Stoic philosophy is divided into three interconnected parts — logic, physics, and ethics — illustrated by an analogy in which physics is the soul, logic the bones and sinews, and ethics the flesh. [SEP: Stoicism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/stoicism/)
- The Stoics held that "virtue is the only good," with external things like wealth and health being morally neutral ("adiaphora"), valuable only as "material for virtue to act upon." [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)
- The Stoic telos (goal) is "living in agreement with nature" — attuning one's own reason with the cosmic reason (logos/Zeus) that pervades and rationally orders the universe. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism); [SEP: Stoicism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/stoicism/)
- The dichotomy of control holds that "assent is 'in our power' or 'up to us' in a way that receiving an impression is not" — i.e., we control our judgments and decisions but not external events or the impressions that first strike us. [SEP: Stoicism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/stoicism/)
- The Stoic sage achieves apatheia — freedom from destructive passions through rational judgment — while still experiencing proper emotional responses called eupatheia. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)
- Roman Stoics: Seneca taught that "virtue is sufficient for happiness," making sages resilient to misfortune; Epictetus taught that people's actions and reactions (but not external events) lie within their control; Marcus Aurelius was a Roman emperor who practiced and exemplified Stoic principles. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)
- Stoicism's legacy extends to modern cognitive behavioral therapy, whose "philosophical origins...can be traced back to the Stoic philosophers." [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)

### Epicureanism

- Epicurus (341–270 BCE, per SEP; 342/1 BCE per Wikipedia) founded his school in Athens around 307/306 BCE, purchasing a property that became known as "the Garden," later used as the name for the school itself. [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism); [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- The Garden was a moderately ascetic community notable for including women and slaves — cosmopolitan for its time — and held monthly social gatherings called Eikas; friendship was central to Epicurean happiness. [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism)
- Epicurus declared "a wise man would feel the torture of a friend no less than his own, and would die for a friend rather than betray him"; friendship arose from utility but came to be "chosen for its own sake." [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- Epicurean pleasure is defined as "the absence of pain in the body and of trouble in the soul," not indulgence; the goal is ataraxia (tranquility/freedom from fear) and aponia (absence of bodily pain). [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism)
- Epicurus distinguished catastematic (static) pleasure — "the pleasure that accompanies well-being as such" — from kinetic pleasure requiring constant new gratification, and held that "pleasure...is not augmented by duration." [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- Desires are classified into three categories: natural and necessary (food, shelter, friendship), natural but unnecessary (luxuries), and unnatural and unnecessary (wealth, power, fame — to be avoided); "empty desires" pursue impossible things like immortality or unlimited wealth. [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism); [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- The Tetrapharmakos ("four-part cure") summarizes Epicurean ethics: don't fear the gods, don't fear death, desires can be easily satisfied, pain is easy to endure. [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism)
- Epicurean physics adopted atomism from Democritus: the universe consists of atoms (possessing only shape, size, and weight) and void; atoms can randomly "swerve," which Epicurus used to explain free will. [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism)
- Per SEP, atoms in Epicurus's system "all move at the same velocity (the principle of isotakheia)," which addressed entropy concerns since "the universe can never come to a halt." [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- Epicurus argued death should not be feared because "while we exist, our death is not, and when our death occurs, we do not exist"; the soul is corporeal and cannot survive bodily death. [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism); [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- Epicurus affirmed the gods exist (via images called simulacra) but held they take no interest in human affairs, since involvement "would compromise their beatitude" — removing anxiety about divine punishment. [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)
- The modern popular meaning of "epicurean" (luxurious indulgence) inverts the historical teaching: Epicurus lived simply and considered excess contrary to peace of mind, being "content with little" and "frugal of vicious indulgence, and fruitful of virtue." [Wikipedia: Epicureanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism); [SEP: Epicurus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epicurus/)

### Skepticism: Pyrrhonism and Academic Skepticism

- Pyrrhonism is named for Pyrrho of Elis (4th century BCE), though the school was formally founded by Aenesidemus in the 1st century BCE; Pyrrho's own recorded views (via Timon) may have been "much more dogmatic than that of the later school." [Wikipedia: Pyrrhonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrhonism)
- Central Pyrrhonist practice is epoché (suspension of judgment), reached by setting opposing arguments against each other to an impasse; the goal is ataraxia, an "untroubled and tranquil condition of soul" that follows from suspending judgment. [Wikipedia: Pyrrhonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrhonism)
- The "Ten Modes of Aenesidemus" are systematic arguments for why certainty is impossible (e.g., perceptual variation among species/individuals, relativity, cultural differences in belief). [Wikipedia: Pyrrhonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrhonism)
- Sextus Empiricus (late 2nd/early 3rd century CE) preserved the main surviving Pyrrhonist texts and added "Agrippa's Five Modes," addressing infinite regress, circularity, and unproven assumptions. [Wikipedia: Pyrrhonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrhonism)
- Academic Skepticism flourished within Plato's Academy from roughly 266 BCE to 90 BCE, beginning when Arcesilaus became scholarch. [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)
- Arcesilaus attacked the Stoic concept of katalepsis (cognitive/convincing impressions yielding certainty), declaring "I cannot know even whether I know or not." [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)
- Carneades, the most influential Academic skeptic, developed acatalepsia (denial of any necessary connection between perceptions and external objects) and proposed a three-tiered scale of plausibility for guiding action without certainty. [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)
- Academic Skeptics held that certain knowledge is impossible but stopped short of nihilism, allowing "degrees of plausibility, and hence degrees of belief, which allow one to act." [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)
- Philo of Larissa was the final major Academic Skeptic proponent before Antiochus of Ascalon rejected skepticism within the Academy. [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)
- Ancient sources distinguished the two skeptical schools: per Aulus Gellius, "the Academics apprehend (in some sense) the very fact that nothing can be apprehended... whereas the Pyrrhonists assert that not even that seems to be true" — Academics focused on critiquing rival schools (especially Stoicism), while Pyrrhonists suspended judgment universally in pursuit of ataraxia. [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism); [Wikipedia: Pyrrhonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrhonism)
- Cicero's *Academica* is the most extensive surviving ancient source on Academic Skepticism, as Cicero himself practiced this form of skepticism. [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)

### Socratic Descent Claimed by Multiple Schools

- Cynicism traced its origin to Antisthenes, a direct student of Socrates. [Wikipedia: Cynicism (philosophy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy))
- Stoicism traced its lineage back to Socrates via Antisthenes and the Cynics (through Crates of Thebes, who taught Zeno), positioning it as a Socratic-descended school as well. [Wikipedia: Stoicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism)
- The Academy (source of Academic Skepticism) was Plato's own school, founded in direct succession from Socrates' teaching, giving it its own claim to Socratic descent distinct from the Cynic/Stoic line. [Wikipedia: Academic Skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_Skepticism)

### Neoplatonism and the Handoff into Late Antiquity/Christianity

- Plotinus (c. 205–270 AD) is "widely considered the father of Neoplatonism," a tradition that emerged in the 3rd century AD and continued through the 6th century. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- Neoplatonism developed through phases after Plotinus: Porphyry's period, Iamblichus's Syrian school, and academies at Alexandria and Athens flourishing in the 5th–6th centuries. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- Core doctrine: "the One" is the supreme, transcendent principle, "so simple that it cannot even be said to exist or to be a being," serving as both the source and teleological endpoint of all reality. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- From the One emanates Nous (Divine Intellect), understood as "simultaneously both being and thought, idea and ideal world"; from Nous emanates the World-Soul, which actualizes the sensible realm — a hierarchy of decreasing perfection. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- Neoplatonism treats evil not as an independent existing thing but as privation — "the absence of good," analogous to darkness as an absence of light. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- The tradition synthesized Middle Platonism and Neopythagoreanism, with Porphyry incorporating Aristotelian logic into the curriculum, and later academies seeking to "harmonise Plato, Aristotle, and — often — the Stoics." [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- Augustine used Neoplatonic philosophy as an intellectual bridge from Manichaeism to Christianity, notably adopting its view that evil is a privation of good rather than a substantial thing. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)
- Origen and Pseudo-Dionysius integrated Neoplatonic thought into Christian theology, shaping contemplative practice in both Eastern Orthodox and Western Christian traditions. [Wikipedia: Neoplatonism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism)

## Part 4 — After antiquity: medieval through the 20th century (survey for sequencing)

### Standard Periodization

- Ancient philosophy runs from early Greek thought (~625 BC) through the Roman period, ending as the Western Roman Empire falls, and is followed by the Middle Ages, Renaissance, Modern, and Contemporary periods in the conventional Western scheme [Wikipedia: Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy).
- Medieval philosophy is standardly dated from the fall of the Western Roman Empire (5th century) to after the Renaissance (13th–14th centuries), divided into an Early Middle Ages phase (to the 12th century) and a "golden age" High Middle Ages phase (12th–14th centuries) marked by recovery of ancient Greek texts [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy).
- The Stanford Encyclopedia notes competing start dates for medieval philosophy (c. 500, 2nd century, or c. 800) and end dates (traditionally 1500, though some scholars extend it to c. 1700 when Aristotelianism faded from universities), underscoring that periodization is itself a scholarly choice [SEP: Medieval Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).
- Renaissance philosophy (roughly 14th–16th centuries) is a transitional period marked by recovery of ancient Greek texts and a humanist shift away from medieval logic/metaphysics toward morality and human virtue [Wikipedia: Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy).
- Modern philosophy conventionally begins in the 17th century and runs into the early 20th century, encompassing Rationalism and Empiricism (17th–18th c.), Kant's critical synthesis (late 18th c.), German Idealism (late 18th–early 19th c.), and late-modern 19th-century reactions [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- Contemporary (20th-century) philosophy's beginning is conventionally marked by Husserl's *Logical Investigations* (1900–01) and Russell's *The Principles of Mathematics* (1903), after which it splits into analytic and continental traditions [Wikipedia: Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy).

### Medieval Philosophy

- Augustine (354–430) synthesized Platonism with Christian theology, held that philosophy must remain subordinate to faith, and anticipated Descartes's cogito with the formulation "if I err therefore I exist"; his work dominated medieval thought for over a millennium [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy).
- The SEP entry calls Augustine "the greatest influence on medieval Latin philosophy," shaped in part by Plotinus and Porphyry [SEP: Medieval Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).
- Boethius (c. 476–c. 525) transmitted Aristotelian logic to the medieval world via translations and commentaries, and his *Consolation of Philosophy* framed medieval education for centuries [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy); [SEP: Medieval Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).
- Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) incorporated Aristotelian philosophy into Christian doctrine, representing the scholastic synthesis of reason and revelation at its apex, with "almost unlimited confidence that Aristotle had reached demonstrative conclusions" compatible with Christian teaching [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy); [SEP: Medieval Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).
- Scholasticism rested on three principles: using logic/dialectic to discover truth (ratio), deferring to ancient authorities like Aristotle (auctoritas), and reconciling philosophy with theological revelation (concordia), typically enacted via the "Question Commentary" format in medieval universities [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy); [SEP: Medieval Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).
- Other notable medieval figures include Anselm and William of Ockham, plus Bonaventure (defender of the Augustinian-Platonic tradition) and Duns Scotus (nominalist) [Wikipedia: Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy); [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy).
- Central medieval concerns were faith versus reason, arguments for God's existence, the problem of universals, and the scope/limits of human knowledge [Wikipedia: Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy).

### Renaissance / Early Modern

- Renaissance philosophy emphasized human dignity and opposed scholasticism; Galileo Galilei founded the scientific method by combining experimentation with mathematics [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- René Descartes (17th c.) pioneered methodic doubt and established "I think, therefore I am" (cogito) as the foundation of certain knowledge, launching rationalism [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- Baruch Spinoza and Gottfried Leibniz developed systematic rationalist metaphysics in the wake of Descartes [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- John Locke, George Berkeley, and David Hume (British empiricists, 17th–18th c.) held that knowledge originates in sensory experience rather than innate ideas, opposing rationalism [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).

### Kant as Turning Point

- Immanuel Kant (late 18th c.) attempted to synthesize rationalism and empiricism through his "critical philosophy," marking a hinge point in modern philosophy [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).

### 19th Century

- G.W.F. Hegel led German Idealism, holding that "the real is rational; the rational is real," developing a systematic dialectical philosophy of history and spirit [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- Karl Marx transformed Hegelian idealism into a materialist philosophy of history (historical materialism) [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855) rejected systematic philosophy as inadequate for lived existence and is generally considered the first existentialist philosopher, emphasizing that individuals alone must create meaning and live authentically [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy); [Wikipedia: Existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism).
- Arthur Schopenhauer advanced a philosophy of pessimism and atheism [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- Friedrich Nietzsche proclaimed "God is dead" and rejected the idea of fixed, universal truth [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- John Stuart Mill advanced utilitarian ethics [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy).
- Pragmatism emerged in the United States around 1870, with Charles Sanders Peirce, William James, and John Dewey as its principal architects; James first used the term "pragmatism" in print in 1898, crediting Peirce with coining it in the early 1870s [Wikipedia: Pragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pragmatism).
- Pragmatism treats language and thought as tools for prediction, problem-solving, and action rather than as mirrors of reality [Wikipedia: Pragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pragmatism).
- William James (1842–1910), an influential psychologist and theorist of religion, became the public face of pragmatism through accessible writing on evaluating beliefs by their practical consequences [Wikipedia: Pragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pragmatism).
- John Dewey (1859–1952), a philosopher of education, championed "instrumentalism" and applied pragmatism to education and democratic life [Wikipedia: Pragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pragmatism).

### 20th Century — Analytic/Continental Split

- Analytic and continental philosophy share the common Western tradition up to Kant but diverge afterward; analytic philosophy developed from Gottlob Frege and Bertrand Russell's work on logic and language, while continental philosophy grew from Husserl's phenomenological method [Wikipedia: Contemporary philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy).
- Analytic philosophy dominates in the UK, Canada, Australia, and the English-speaking world generally, while continental philosophy prevails in continental Europe (Germany, France, Italy, Spain, Portugal) [Wikipedia: Contemporary philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy).
- Bertrand Russell and G.E. Moore pioneered analytic philosophy's emphasis on logical clarity, with Russell's "On Denoting" exemplifying its precise, linguistic method [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy); [Wikipedia: Contemporary philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy).
- Phenomenology, founded by Edmund Husserl around 1900, is the systematic study of consciousness and phenomena and is the foundational starting point for 20th-century continental philosophy [Wikipedia: Contemporary philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy).
- Martin Heidegger (1889–1976), through *Being and Time* (1927), grounded philosophical analysis in human existence (*Dasein*) and provided conceptual foundations that influenced Sartre; existentialism emerged from phenomenology, emphasizing human existence and meaning in an apparently meaningless world [Wikipedia: Existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism); [Wikipedia: Contemporary philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy).
- Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980) is the most prominent existentialist figure; his 1945 lecture *Existentialism Is a Humanism* popularized the movement, and he defined it around the idea that "existence precedes essence" — humans create themselves through choices rather than possessing a predetermined nature [Wikipedia: Existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism).
- Simone de Beauvoir integrated existentialism with feminist philosophy (e.g., *The Second Sex*), and Albert Camus explored how individuals must persist meaningfully despite life's absurdity ("one must imagine Sisyphus happy"), though he resisted the existentialist label [Wikipedia: Existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism).
- Ludwig Wittgenstein's career divides into two phases: the early *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*, which pursued logical atomism and held that the logical relationship between propositions and the world could resolve philosophical problems; and the later *Philosophical Investigations*, which argued instead that word-meaning is best understood as use within a "language game" [Wikipedia: Ludwig Wittgenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein).
- Wittgenstein pioneered the "linguistic turn" in analytic philosophy, treating many traditional philosophical puzzles as arising from confusions about language, and his shift from systematic logic to a "therapeutic" view of philosophical problems influenced logic, epistemology, aesthetics, and philosophy of religion [Wikipedia: Ludwig Wittgenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein).

### Notes on Sequencing / Canonicity

- Across sources, the figures repeatedly flagged as anchor points for a period-by-period intro sequence are: Augustine and Aquinas (medieval); Descartes, Locke/Berkeley/Hume, and Kant (early modern turning point); Hegel, Marx, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, and Mill (19th century); and Husserl, Heidegger, Sartre, Wittgenstein, and James/Dewey (20th century, split across continental existentialism/phenomenology, analytic philosophy, and American pragmatism) — these are the names given explicit standalone treatment across the fetched pages, distinguishing them from secondary figures mentioned only in passing (e.g., Anselm, Ockham, Bonaventure, Duns Scotus, Fichte, Schelling, Schopenhauer, Beauvoir, Camus, Frege, Russell, Moore) [Wikipedia: Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy); [Wikipedia: Medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy); [Wikipedia: Modern philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_philosophy); [Wikipedia: Contemporary philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_philosophy).
- The SEP medieval-philosophy entry's excerpt did not explicitly rank figures by "essential for an intro sequence"; that framing is inferred from which figures receive dedicated treatment versus passing mention [SEP: Medieval Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).

## Part 5 — How the history of philosophy is taught to beginners

### Sequencing Conventions: Chronological vs. Problems-Based

- Introductory philosophy courses and textbooks are commonly designed around one of two structures: a **historical/chronological approach** (organized by period and thinker) or a **problems-based approach** (organized by philosophical question — e.g., free will, knowledge, ethics — pulling arguments from across eras) [Historical vs. problems approaches, OUP](https://global.oup.com/ushe/product/a-historical-introduction-to-philosophy-9780195139846).
- The historical approach is described as giving students "a better sense of the drama of philosophy and a much richer picture of the evolution of...concepts," and is considered the natural fit for courses explicitly labeled "History of Philosophy" [Historiography and the History of Philosophy, OpenStax](https://openstax.org/books/introduction-philosophy/pages/4-1-historiography-and-the-history-of-philosophy).
- Many contemporary intro textbooks use a **hybrid**: presenting philosophy as a living discipline defined by recurring problems, made intelligible by tracing their historical roots (e.g., organizing around two key historical periods — Ancient Greece and Early Modernity — alongside thematic chapters on mind, science, ethics, politics) [Introduction to Philosophy, Open Textbook Library](https://open.umn.edu/opentextbooks/textbooks/introduction-to-philosophy).
- MIT's "Problems of Philosophy" course (24.00) is an explicit example of the problems-based structure at the intro level, distinct from MIT's separate "Ancient Philosophy" (24.200) and "Classics of Western Philosophy" (24.01) courses, which are historically/chronologically organized [MIT OCW Problems of Philosophy](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/24-00-problems-of-philosophy-fall-2019/).
- Russell's own *A History of Western Philosophy* (1945 US / 1946 UK) exemplifies the chronological convention: it runs from pre-Socratics (Thales, Pythagoras) through 20th-century figures (William James, Dewey), divided into three blocks — Ancient Philosophy, Catholic Philosophy, Modern Philosophy — with each chapter typically devoted to one philosopher or school [History of Western Philosophy (Russell), Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Western_Philosophy_(Russell)).
- Wikipedia's "Outline of Philosophy" itself layers multiple organizing schemes simultaneously — by branch (metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, aesthetics, logic), by region, and by historical period — showing both conventions coexist in general reference works [Outline of Philosophy, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_philosophy).

### Where Courses Conventionally Start

- University "Ancient Philosophy" / "History of Ancient Philosophy" courses conventionally open with the **Presocratic natural philosophers** active in Ionia in the 6th century BCE — Thales, Anaximander, Anaximenes — then Heraclitus, Parmenides, Anaxagoras, and Democritus, before reaching Socrates [UCSD ancient philosophy syllabus](https://philosophy.ucsd.edu/_files/courses/24fall/Phil%20110%20Shields%20Christopher).
- A common course scope is roughly 585 BCE to 100 BCE, moving Presocratics to Socrates to Plato to Aristotle to Hellenistic schools (Epicureans, early Stoics), often with one syllabus module per figure/school [Katja Vogt intro ancient philosophy syllabus](https://katjavogt.github.io/wp-content/uploads/syllabus-vogt-ancient-intro-2020.pdf).
- Within that arc, Socrates is conventionally framed as the pivot who "emphasized ethical questions" in contrast to the Presocratics' focus on nature/cosmology, with his pupil Plato and Plato's pupil Aristotle developing the era's systematic philosophy [UCSD ancient philosophy syllabus](https://philosophy.ucsd.edu/_files/courses/24fall/Phil%20110%20Shields%20Christopher).
- Some syllabi begin the study of Plato conventionally *with the Euthyphro* specifically, before moving to longer dialogues [Hawaii Phil 211 syllabus](http://www2.hawaii.edu/~freeman/courses/phil211/syllabus.pdf).
- MIT's "Ancient Philosophy" (24.200, Fall 2004) sequences the trial-and-death dialogues early in the term: the Apology and Crito in Session 3 ("Socrates and the Law"), the Euthyphro in Session 4 ("Socratic Method") [MIT OCW 24.200 Readings](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/24-200-ancient-philosophy-fall-2004/pages/readings/).
- MIT also runs a dedicated "Classics of Western Philosophy" course (24.01) with study questions specifically packaged for the Apology, Crito, and Euthyphro as a unit [MIT OCW 24.01 Study Questions](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/24-01-classics-of-western-philosophy-spring-2016/resources/mit24_01s16_ses2/).

### Why Euthyphro / Apology / Crito Are the Standard First Readings for Socrates

- These three (often with the Phaedo as a fourth) recount a single continuous narrative arc — Socrates' trial and execution in 399 BC — giving beginners a self-contained story rather than an isolated abstract argument [Euthyphro, Apology, Crito, Phaedo overview, CliffsNotes](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/literature/e/euthyphro-apology-crito-and-phaedo/about-euthyphro-apology-crito-and-phaedo).
- In the Euthyphro (set just before the trial), Socrates and Euthyphro debate "What is piety/holiness?"; in the Apology, Socrates defends himself against the charges at trial; in the Crito (set in prison the night before execution), Socrates explains why he won't escape and break the law [Euthyphro, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euthyphro); [Apology (Plato), Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apology_(Plato)).
- This grouping has ancient precedent: in the 1st century AD, Thrasyllus of Mendes' canonical ordering of Plato's works placed the Euthyphro first in his first tetralogy, followed by the Apology, Crito, and Phaedo — and some ancient teachers reportedly used the Euthyphro as literally the first dialogue in their courses, viewing it as the most suitable introduction to Plato [Euthyphro, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euthyphro).
- These are grouped in Plato scholarship under "Plato's Shorter Ethical Works" — the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy has a dedicated entry treating them as a class distinguished by brevity and focus on a single ethical question (define X: piety, courage, temperance, etc.) [Plato's Shorter Ethical Works, SEP](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato-ethics-shorter/).
- Practically, their short length relative to Plato's longer, more technical dialogues (Republic, Timaeus, Parmenides) is repeatedly cited as what makes them tractable as first assigned readings for students new to philosophy [Bryn Mawr Classical Review](https://bmcr.brynmawr.edu/2005/2005.09.65/).

### Canonical Beginner Histories/Surveys

- **Bertrand Russell, *A History of Western Philosophy*** (1945/46): written by the philosopher-logician (1872–1970) during WWII; spans Thales to Dewey/James chronologically. Reviewers (per A.C. Grayling) called it "beautifully written, witty, clear, lucid and magisterial" but "not always accurate...nor always fair," with its ancient/medieval sections criticized as scholarship as "nearly worthless"; it has remained continuously in print and reached wide popular readership, but per Grayling "has never been a staple as an academic textbook" [History of Western Philosophy (Russell), Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Western_Philosophy_(Russell)).
- **Anthony Kenny, *A New History of Western Philosophy*** (Oxford University Press): a four-volume set — *Ancient Philosophy*, *Medieval Philosophy*, *Rise of Modern Philosophy*, *Modern Philosophy* — "specially written for a broad popular readership but serious and deep enough" for genuine understanding; tells the story chronologically while also tracking themes across the volumes [A New History of Western Philosophy, OUP/Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/New-History-Western-Philosophy-Four/dp/0199561400).
- **Jostein Gaarder, *Sophie's World*** (Norwegian, 1991; English translation 1995): a novel in which teenager Sophie Amundsen receives letters from a mystery philosopher taking her through Western philosophy from the pre-Socratics to Sartre; its nonfictional philosophical content "roughly aligns with" Russell's *History*; it became the best-selling book in the world in 1995 and had sold over 40 million copies in 59 languages by 2011 [Sophie's World, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophie's_World).

### Notable Free/Popular On-Ramps

- **"History of Philosophy Without Any Gaps"** — a podcast/book series by Peter Adamson (professor at LMU Munich and King's College London), which proceeds without skipping minor figures, covering the emergence of philosophy with the Presocratics, Socrates' probing questions, then Plato's dialogues and Aristotle's treatises; it surpassed 25 million downloads by 2019 and has since expanded into companion series on Indian, Africana, and Chinese philosophy [History of Philosophy Without Any Gaps](https://historyofphilosophy.net/); [Peter Adamson, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Adamson_(philosopher)).
- **Crash Course Philosophy** (YouTube, 2016) — 46 free episodes hosted by Hank Green, explicitly "based on an introductory Western philosophy college-level curriculum," opening with philosophy's Greek origins and its three traditional divisions (metaphysics, epistemology, value theory) [Crash Course Philosophy, Open Culture](https://www.openculture.com/2016/02/crash-course-philosophy-hank-greens-fast-paced-introduction-to-philosophy-gets-underway-on-youtube.html).
- **MIT OpenCourseWare** publishes full free syllabi, readings, and assignments for its Ancient Philosophy (24.200) and Classics of Western Philosophy (24.01) courses, usable as a self-study sequence [MIT OCW Ancient Philosophy](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/24-200-ancient-philosophy-fall-2004/).

### Free/Authoritative Reference Works

- **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP)**: free, maintained by Stanford University; every entry (new or substantially revised) goes through a peer-review process "similar to those used by academic journals," and each entry is written/maintained by a subject-matter expert [SEP, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Encyclopedia_of_Philosophy); [SEP, NEH](https://www.neh.gov/project/stanford-encyclopedia-philosophy).
- **Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy (IEP)**: also free, but runs on an invited-contributor model without SEP's formal peer-review standard; some academic reviewers weight IEP articles as less authoritative for this reason, though it's noted for covering topics/gaps SEP does not [Philosophy of Science Portal: IEP vs SEP](http://philosophyofscienceportal.blogspot.com/2013/12/internet-encyclopedia-of-philosophy-iep.html).
- **Perseus Digital Library (Tufts University)**: hosts Ancient Greek texts including Plato's dialogues with parallel Greek text and translation; its Plato texts derive from John Burnet's Oxford University Press critical edition (1903) [Perseus Digital Library, Plato collection](https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/collection?collection=Perseus:corpus:perseus,author,Plato).
- **Project Gutenberg**: hosts free public-domain ebooks of Benjamin Jowett's classic English translations of Plato's *Euthyphro*, *Apology*, plus a combined volume *Apology, Crito, and Phaedo of Socrates* [Euthyphro, Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1642); [Apology, Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/1656); [Apology, Crito, and Phaedo, Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/13726).
