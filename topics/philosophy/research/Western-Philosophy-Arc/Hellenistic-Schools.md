# Hellenistic Schools

The schools after Aristotle (conventionally 323 BC, Alexander's death, to 31 BC), when philosophy
turned practical and therapeutic — an "art of living" seeking inner security as the independent
polis collapsed.

## The schools

- **Cynicism** — Antisthenes (a student of Socrates) and above all Diogenes of Sinope: virtue as
  the sole necessity, radical asceticism, rejection of convention. Crates of Thebes taught Zeno,
  linking Cynicism directly to Stoicism's origin.
- **Stoicism** — Zeno of Citium, ~300 BC, at the Stoa Poikile; systematized by Chrysippus; the
  surviving complete texts are Roman (Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius). Virtue is the only
  good (externals are *adiaphora*); the telos is living in agreement with nature/the cosmic
  logos; the dichotomy of control (our judgments are up to us, externals are not); the sage's
  *apatheia*. Modern cognitive behavioral therapy traces its philosophical origins here.
- **Epicureanism** — Epicurus, the Garden (~307/306 BC), a community that included women and
  slaves. Pleasure defined as absence of pain (*ataraxia*, *aponia*), not indulgence — the modern
  sense of "epicurean" inverts the teaching. Desire taxonomy (natural-necessary / natural-unnecessary /
  empty); the Tetrapharmakos; Democritean atomism with the "swerve" for free will; death is
  nothing to us; gods exist but don't intervene.
- **Skepticism** — two branches: Pyrrhonism (Pyrrho; formalized by Aenesidemus; preserved by
  Sextus Empiricus) practicing *epoché* (suspension of judgment) toward *ataraxia*; and Academic
  Skepticism (Arcesilaus, Carneades) inside Plato's own Academy, attacking Stoic certainty while
  allowing degrees of plausibility for action.
- **Neoplatonism** — Plotinus (3rd c. AD): the One → Nous → World-Soul emanation hierarchy; evil
  as privation. The bridge into Christian thought (Augustine, Origen, Pseudo-Dionysius).

## Socratic descent

Multiple schools claimed Socrates as ancestor: Cynics via Antisthenes; Stoics via the Cynic line
to Zeno; the skeptical Academy via Plato's own school. See [[_grounding]] Part 3.
