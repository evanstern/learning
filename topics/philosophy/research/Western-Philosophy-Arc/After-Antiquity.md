# After Antiquity

Survey-level map of Western philosophy after the ancient period — one tier down only, for
sequencing later lessons in this series.

## Periodization

Conventional scheme: Ancient (to the fall of the Western Roman Empire) → Medieval (5th–15th c.,
with scholarly disagreement on exact boundaries) → Renaissance (14th–16th c.) → Modern (17th–early
20th c.) → Contemporary (20th c. on, conventionally marked by Husserl 1900–01 and Russell 1903,
splitting into analytic and continental traditions). See [[_grounding]] Part 4.

## Anchor figures by period

- **Medieval:** Augustine (Platonism + Christianity; "if I err, I exist"), Boethius (transmitted
  Aristotle's logic), Aquinas (Aristotle + Christian doctrine; the scholastic apex). Scholastic
  method: ratio, auctoritas, concordia. Core concerns: faith vs reason, God's existence, the
  problem of universals. Secondary: Anselm, Ockham, Bonaventure, Duns Scotus.
- **Early modern:** Descartes (methodic doubt, cogito — rationalism), Spinoza, Leibniz; the
  British empiricists Locke, Berkeley, Hume; **Kant** as the hinge synthesizing rationalism and
  empiricism.
- **19th century:** Hegel (German Idealism), Marx (historical materialism), Kierkegaard (first
  existentialist), Nietzsche ("God is dead"), Mill (utilitarianism), Schopenhauer; American
  pragmatism (Peirce, James, Dewey).
- **20th century:** the analytic/continental split — Frege/Russell/Moore and Wittgenstein
  (Tractatus → Philosophical Investigations; the linguistic turn) on one side; Husserl's
  phenomenology, Heidegger, Sartre/Beauvoir/Camus (existentialism) on the other.

The "anchor" status of these figures is inferred from which receive dedicated treatment across
the fetched period overviews, not from an explicit ranking in any single source. See
[[_grounding]] Part 4.
