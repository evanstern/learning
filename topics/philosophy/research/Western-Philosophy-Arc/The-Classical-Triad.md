# The Classical Triad

Survey of the three-generation lineage — Socrates → Plato → Aristotle — that dominates classical
Greek philosophy. (Socrates gets his own lesson-scope deep dive; here he is arc context.)

## Socrates as the hinge

Cicero's summary: Socrates "brought philosophy down from the heavens, placed it in cities,
introduced it into families" — the turn from cosmology to ethics that splits Greek philosophy
into pre-Socratic and classical periods. Plato was his student and chief chronicler; Plato taught
Aristotle at the Academy from around 367 BC. See [[_grounding]] Part 2.

## Plato (c. 428–347 BC)

Founded the Academy (~383 BC); invented the philosophical dialogue as a form, with Socrates as
speaker — Plato never speaks in his own voice, and the relation of his literary Socrates to the
historical one is contested. Central doctrine: the theory of Forms — the changing sensory world
vs an eternal, unchanging realm of Forms grasped by reason, with particulars "participating" in
them. In the *Republic*: the tripartite soul (reason/spirit/appetite) mirroring the city's three
classes, justice as each part doing its proper work, philosopher-kings, the allegory of the cave,
and the decline sequence of regimes. Dialogues are conventionally grouped early (aporetic,
ethical — Apology, Crito, Euthyphro), middle (Forms — Republic, Phaedo, Symposium), late (Laws,
Timaeus); beginners are standardly pointed at the short early dialogues first, with the Apology
regarded as the most historically reliable source on Socrates. See [[_grounding]] Part 2.

## Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Twenty years in Plato's Academy, then founded the Lyceum (335 BC; the "Peripatetics"). Broke with
Plato on the Forms: universals exist only as instantiated in particular things. Distinctly
empirical — roughly two years of systematic biological observation on Lesbos. Key apparatus: the
four causes (material, formal, efficient, final); syllogistic logic (the *Organon*), dominant in
the West until the 19th century; virtue ethics in the *Nicomachean Ethics* — excellence as a
habituated mean between extremes, the good life as activity in accordance with excellence over a
complete life. About a third of his output survives, mostly as lecture texts; the *Nicomachean
Ethics* and *Physics* are the most-discussed entry points, though no canonical beginner order
exists as it does for Plato. See [[_grounding]] Part 2.
